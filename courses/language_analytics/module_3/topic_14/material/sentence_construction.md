# Sentence Construction

## Quick Revision: Subject–Verb Agreement

| **Rule** | **Example** | **Explanation** |
|---------|-------------|-----------------|
| *Singular subject → Singular verb* | *The manager **approves** the proposal after reviewing all the data.* | *Singular subject → takes a **singular verb***. |
| *Plural subject → Plural verb* | *The researchers **analyze** the results before publishing the report.* | *Plural subject → takes a **plural verb***. |
| *Use of "has / have"* | *The company **has** implemented several innovative strategies this year.<br>You **have** demonstrated excellent communication skills.* | _Singular subject → **has**.<br>Plural subject / I, you → **have**._ |
| *Negative sentences (do / does)* | *She **does not** agree with the revised contract terms.<br>They **do not** believe the system will function efficiently.* | _Singular subject → **does not**.<br>Plural subject → **do not**._ |
| *Questions (do / does)* | ***Does** the new software support multilingual interfaces?<br>**Do** the students need additional resources?* | _Singular subject → **does**.<br>Plural subject → **do**._ |
| *Here / There / This / That / These / Those* | *There **is** a significant improvement in performance.<br>Here **are** the documents you requested.<br>This **is** the prototype we developed.<br>Those **are** the concerns raised by the QC department.* | _Verb agrees with the real subject that follows._ |
| *Hypothetical sentences (use "were")* | *I wish the schedule **were** more flexible.<br>If the engineer **were** available, the issue could be solved sooner.* | _**Were** is used in imaginary or wishful situations._ |
| *Uncountable subjects → Singular verb* | *The equipment **is** regularly maintained.<br>Detailed feedback **is** essential for improvement.* | _Uncountable nouns → always a **singular verb**._ |
| *Collective nouns as one unit → Singular verb* | *The jury **has** reached a unanimous decision.<br>The team **is** preparing a presentation for the conference.* | _When acting as one unit → **singular verb**._ |
| *Collective nouns as individuals → Plural verb* | *The committee **are** expressing different viewpoints.<br>My family **are** arranging their own schedules.* | _When members act individually → **plural verb**._ |

## Agreement of Subject and Verb

In English, a singular subject normally takes a singular verb, and a plural subject normally takes a plural verb.
However, there are some special cases where the choice of verb is different, depending on the structure and meaning of the sentence.

### Scenario 1: "And" – Dual vs. Unified Subjects

When subjects are joined with "and", the verb usually becomes plural because the subjects are treated as two or more separate entities (a dual subject).
However, sometimes "and" joins words into one single idea (a unified subject), and in that case, the verb is singular.

| **Case** | **Example** | **Explanation / Rule** |
|----------|--------------|--------------------------|
| *Dual Subjects → Plural Verb* | *Rahul and Sita **are** here.* | *Two separate people (dual) → plural verb.* |
| | *Ravi, Meena and Suresh **have** submitted their project reports.* | _Three separate people (dual) → plural verb._ |
| | *Both bread and butter **are** out of stock.* | _"Bread" and "butter" treated as two separate items (dual) → plural verb._ |
| *Unified Subjects → Singular Verb* | *Bread and butter **is** my breakfast.* | _"Bread and butter" is seen as one meal (unified idea) → singular verb._ |

### Scenario 2: "Either/Or" and "Neither/Nor" – Nearest Subject Rule

When subjects are joined with **or**, **nor**, **either…or**, or **neither…nor**,
the verb agrees with the **subject that comes closest to the verb** (the nearest subject).

| **Case** | **Example** | **Explanation / Rule** |
|----------|--------------|--------------------------|
| *Nearest subject is singular → Singular verb* | *Either the senior managers or the director **is** attending the conference.* | _The subject nearest to the verb (**director**) is singular → use a **singular verb**._ |
| | *Neither the students nor the teacher **was** satisfied with the explanation.* | _The nearest subject (**teacher**) is singular → verb is **singular**._ |
| *Nearest subject is plural → Plural verb* | *Either the director or the senior managers **are** attending the conference.* | _The subject nearest to the verb (**senior managers**) is plural → use a **plural verb**._ |
| | *Neither the team leader nor the engineers **have** submitted their final designs.* | _The nearest subject (**engineers**) is plural → verb is **plural**._ |

### Scenario 3: Always Singular Words

Words like **everyone**, **everybody**, **each**, **no one**, **nobody**, **nothing**, **nowhere**, **someone**, **somebody**, **anyone**, **anybody**, and **everything** are **singular in meaning**,
so they always take a **singular verb**.

Everyone = every one person → singular.
No one / nobody / nothing / nowhere = not one → singular.

| **Case** | **Example** | **Explanation / Rule** |
|----------|--------------|--------------------------|
| *Pronouns (everyone, everybody)* | *Everyone **is** expected to follow the laboratory safety guidelines.* | _**Everyone** refers to each individual person → use a **singular verb**._ |
| | *Everybody **was** impressed by her technical presentation.* | _**Everybody** = all persons, but grammatically singular → **singular verb**._ |
| *Each + singular noun / pronoun* | *Each of the students **has** submitted the assignment on the portal.* | _**Each** focuses on individuals one by one → takes a **singular verb**._ |
| | *Each device **requires** regular calibration.* | _**Each device** = every single device → **requires** (singular verb)._ |
| *Negative pronouns (no one, nobody, nothing)* | *No one **was** aware of the sudden system failure.* | _**No one** = not even one person → **singular verb**._ |
| | *Nothing **happens** in this project without proper documentation.* | _**Nothing** refers to a single idea (no thing) → **singular verb**._ |
| *Someone / Somebody / Anyone / Anybody* | *Somebody **has** left their laptop in the seminar hall.* | _**Somebody** is grammatically singular → use a **singular verb**._ |

More examples:

- *Everyone **is** ready to begin the group presentation.*
- *Everybody **runs** along the trail early in the morning to enjoy the fresh air.*
- *Each student in the science fair **has** a unique project that reflects their creativity and research skills.*
- *Nobody **does** the annual financial report better than the experienced accounting manager.*
- *Somebody **has** mistakenly taken my bag while leaving the conference room.*
- *Anybody who **wants** to try this problem can come to the board.*
- *No one **was** present in the classroom when the teacher arrived, because all the students had gone to the auditorium for the event.*
- *Nothing **matters** anymore to him after losing the final match.*
- *Everything **works** perfectly in this new smartphone, from its camera features to its high-speed processor and long-lasting battery.*

### Subject–Verb Agreement with Either and Neither

**Either** = "one or the other" (positive choice).
**Neither** = "not one or the other" (negative choice).

| **Case** | **Example** | **Explanation / Rule** |
|----------|--------------|--------------------------|
| *Neither + singular → singular verb* | *We've got two TVs, but neither **works** properly.* | _**Neither** = "not one" of the two → takes a **singular verb**._ |
| *Neither (two options) → singular* | *We looked at the two suggested answers, but neither **is** right.* | _Refers to "not one of the two answers" → **singular verb**._ |
| *Either (one of two) → singular* | *I solved both questions, but either one **was** enough to pass.* | _**Either** = "one or the other (one question)" → **singular verb**._ |

> **Note on "Either of …"**
> Use **either of** when *either* comes before **the, these, those**, or possessives (**my, your, his, her, their**, etc.) with a plural noun.
> **Either** = "one of two".
> *Example:* *Either of these books **is** useful for the exam.*
> *Example:* *I'll be happy if either of your brothers **helps** me.*
>
> **Note on "Neither of …"**
> Use **neither of** when *neither* comes before **the, these, those**, or possessives (**my, your, his, her, their**, etc.) with a plural noun.
> **Neither** = "not one of two".
> *Example:* *Neither of the options **is** correct.*
> *Example:* *Neither of my friends **was** available yesterday.*

### Formal Usage (Always Singular)

| **Case** | **Example** | **Explanation / Rule** |
|----------|--------------|--------------------------|
| *Either of these* | *Either of these **is** acceptable.* | _**Either of** + plural noun → verb is **singular** in formal English._ |
| *Either of you* | ***Does** either of you have a phone?* | _Auxiliary verb is **singular** (*does*), agreeing with **either**._ |
| *Either of us* | *If either of us **finds** the key, we will **win** the game.* | _**Either** = one of two → main verb is **singular** (*finds*)._ |
| *Neither of the birthday cards* | *Neither of the birthday cards **was** suitable.* | _**Neither of** + plural noun → **singular verb** (*was*) in formal English._ |

> In informal spoken English, you may sometimes hear plural verbs after either of / neither of with plural nouns, although, conventionally, the singular forms of verbs are preferred.
>
> - *Neither of them **were** interested in going.*
> - *Either of these **are** acceptable.*
> - *Neither of my friends **were** available yesterday.*

### Scenario 4: Always Plural Words

Some words are **always plural** because they refer to "more than one". These include words such as **many**, **both**, **few**, and **several**.

They are:

- used **only with countable nouns**
- they **always take a plural verb**

❌ *Both student **is** present.*
✅ *Both students **are** present.*

| **Case** | **Example** | **Explanation / Rule** |
|----------|--------------|--------------------------|
| *Many* | *Many engineers **have** applied for the new software position.* | _**Many** + plural countable noun (**engineers**) → **plural verb** (*have*)._ |
| *Both* | *Both the questions **were** tough.* | _**Both** + plural noun (**questions**) → **plural verb** (*were*)._ |
| *Few* | *Few students **were** interested in the lecture on ancient history.* | _**Few** suggests a small number of countable items (**students**) → **plural verb**._ |
| *Several* | *Several participants **do not** agree with the proposed changes.* | _**Several** + plural noun (**participants**) → **plural verb** (*do not agree*)._ |

### Scenario 5: First Subject Rule

When a singular or plural subject is followed by a group of words such as **as well as**, **along with**, **together with**, **in addition to**,
the **verb must agree with the first subject**, not with the phrase that follows.

| **Group of Words** | **Example** | **Explanation** |
|---------------------|--------------|--------------------|
| *As well as* | *The teacher, as well as the students, **was** present.* | **Teacher** *is singular* → *use* **singular** *verb*. |
| *As well as* | *The students, as well as the teacher, **are** ready.* | **Students** *is plural* → *use* **plural** *verb*. |
| *Along with* | *My friend, along with his parents, **is** visiting today.* | **Friend** *is singular* → *use* **singular** *verb*. |
| *Along with* | *My friends, along with their parents, **are** visiting today.* | **Friends** *is plural* → *use* **plural** *verb*. |
| *Together with* | *The captain, together with his team, **was** honored at the ceremony.* | **Captain** *is singular* → *use* **singular** *verb*. |
| *Together with* | *The captains, together with their teams, **were** honored at the ceremony.* | **Captains** *is plural* → *use* **plural** *verb*. |
| *In addition to* | *The manager, in addition to the assistants, **has** approved the plan.* | **Manager** *is singular* → *use* **singular** *verb*. |
| *In addition to* | *The managers, in addition to the assistants, **have** approved the plan.* | **Managers** *is plural* → *use* **plural** *verb*. |

### Scenario 6: Singular or Plural (Depending on the Subject)

Some subjects with words like **all**, **some**, **most**, **none**, etc. can take a singular or plural verb depending on the noun that follows.

- If the noun is **singular / uncountable**, the verb must be **singular**.
- If the noun is **plural / countable**, the verb must be **plural**.

*All the water **is** gone.* (water = uncountable → singular verb)
*All the students **are** ready.* (students = plural → plural verb)

#### 6.1. All / Some / Most + of + noun

| **Case** | **Example** | **Explanation / Rule** |
|----------|--------------|--------------------------|
| *All of (uncountable)* | *All of the rice **is** cooked.* | _**Rice** is uncountable → use a **singular** verb (*is*)._ |
| *All of (plural)* | *All of the students **are** present.* | _**Students** is plural → use a **plural** verb (*are*)._ |
| *Some of (uncountable)* | *Some of the water **is** left.* | _**Water** is uncountable → **singular** verb._ |
| *Some of (plural)* | *Some of the books **are** missing.* | _**Books** is plural → **plural** verb._ |
| | *Some of the apples **are** red.* | _**Apples** is plural → **plural** verb._ |
| | *Some of the milk **has** gone bad.* | _**Milk** is uncountable → **singular** verb (*has*)._ |
| *Most of (uncountable)* | *Most of the advice **is** helpful.* | _**Advice** is uncountable → **singular** verb._ |
| *Most of (plural)* | *Most of the teachers **are** kind.* | _**Teachers** is plural → **plural** verb._ |

> **Note: "None of" – Singular or Plural?**
>
> - With **uncountable nouns**, use a **singular** verb:
>   _None of the information **has** been updated._
>   _None of the furniture **was** damaged._
>
> - With **plural nouns**, you can use **was** or **were**:
>   _None of the players **was** selected._ ✅ (more formal, exam style)
>   _None of the players **were** selected._ ✅ (very common in everyday English)
>
> For **exams and formal writing**, it is safer to use the **singular** verb.
