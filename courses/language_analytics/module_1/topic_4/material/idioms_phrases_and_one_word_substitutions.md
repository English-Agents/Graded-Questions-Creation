## Why Language Goes Beyond Words: Introduction

Consider the sentence: "After three gruelling rounds of negotiation, the diplomat finally threw in the towel." If you read those last four words literally, you might picture someone flinging a cloth across a conference room. But that is not what happened. The diplomat gave up. This gap between the words on the page and the idea they actually convey is the central challenge, and the central fascination, of three closely related areas of verbal ability: idioms, phrases, and one word substitutions.

Together, these three topics reveal how English compresses meaning. Idioms do it through figurative expression, using a fixed group of words whose collective meaning is unrelated to the individual words. Phrasal verbs do it through combination, pairing a verb with a particle that transforms the verb's meaning entirely. One word substitutions do the opposite. They compress a long, descriptive phrase into a single precise term.

<MultiLineNote>
<b>Why These Three Topics Matter in Examinations</b><br><br>
Idioms, phrasal verbs, and one word substitutions appear in virtually every competitive English examination, from school-level assessments to national entrance tests. They test not just vocabulary, but your understanding of how language actually works: idiomatically, economically, and contextually. Students who treat these as lists to memorise invariably underperform. Students who understand the underlying logic consistently score higher.
</MultiLineNote>
</Section>

##  Idioms

### Definition and Core Idea

| **Term** | **Definition** |
| --- | --- |
| Idiom | A fixed group of words whose overall meaning cannot be deduced from the literal meaning of the individual words within it. |

Idioms are the personality of a language. They carry cultural history, lived experience, and layers of implied meaning that no dictionary definition can fully reproduce. When a sailor historically "knew the ropes" on a ship, they were competent and experienced. That expression carried its meaning into everyday life long after the sailing context disappeared. This is how idioms work: their origins are often literal, but their current usage is entirely figurative.

<MultiLineNote>

<b>Origin Spotlight: Understanding Idioms Through Their Roots</b><br><br>
<b>'Bite the bullet':</b> One popular theory suggests that in the era before anaesthesia, soldiers undergoing surgery were given a bullet to bite down on to endure the pain. Whether or not this origin is historically verified, the idiom today means to endure a painful or difficult situation without complaint.<br><br>
<b>'Under the weather':</b> One theory suggests that sailors who felt ill were sent below deck to avoid the harsh weather conditions above. The expression today simply means feeling unwell.
</MultiLineNote>

Understanding an idiom's origin is one of the most effective ways to remember its meaning permanently. When you next encounter an unfamiliar idiom, try to trace its historical or contextual roots. Even a plausible story you construct yourself will help the expression stick.

###  How Idiom Questions Are Worded

| **Type** | **Instruction Wording** | **What Is Expected** |
| --- | --- | --- |
| Type 1 | 'Choose the correct meaning of the given idiom.' | Know the idiom's figurative meaning and select the option that best captures it. |
| Type 2 | 'Choose the sentence in which the idiom is used correctly.' | Evaluate whether each sentence uses the idiom in a contextually appropriate and grammatically correct way. |
| Type 3 | 'Replace the underlined phrase with the correct idiom.' | Find the idiom whose figurative meaning matches the literal description underlined in the sentence. |

Type 2 is the most demanding because it requires both knowledge of meaning and sensitivity to usage. An idiom used in the wrong context, even with the correct figurative meaning, will read awkwardly or be logically contradictory. Always evaluate not just what the idiom means, but whether the surrounding sentence supports that meaning.

###  Five Learning Strategies for Idioms

<SubSection>
#### Strategy 1: Always Learn the Context, Not Just the Phrase

An idiom divorced from its context is almost useless for examination purposes. Every time you learn a new idiom, write a full sentence that correctly illustrates its figurative meaning. The sentence anchors the idiom in a situation, making both the meaning and usage memorable.
</SubSection>

<SubSection>
#### Strategy 2: Group Idioms by Theme

Human experience clusters around universal themes: success and failure, courage and fear, secrecy and disclosure, effort and laziness. Grouping idioms by theme creates a mental map that allows you to retrieve the right expression quickly. For example, idioms about courage include: 'face the music', 'bite the bullet', 'take the bull by the horns', and 'show one's mettle'. Knowing that a question is about courage narrows your recall significantly.

<MultiLineNote>
<b>Thematic Idiom Clusters: Quick Reference</b><br><br>
<b>DECEPTION / SECRECY:</b><br>
&nbsp;•&nbsp; 'Keep something under wraps' (keep secret) 
&nbsp;•&nbsp; 'Pull the wool over someone's eyes' (deceive) 
&nbsp;•&nbsp; 'Have an ace up one's sleeve' (hold a secret advantage)<br><br>
<b>COURAGE / DETERMINATION:</b><br>
&nbsp;•&nbsp; 'Face the music' (accept consequences) 
&nbsp;•&nbsp; 'Burn the midnight oil' (work late) 
&nbsp;•&nbsp; 'Take the bull by the horns' (tackle a problem directly)<br><br>
<b>DIFFICULTY / FAILURE:</b><br>
&nbsp;•&nbsp; 'Go back to square one' (start over) 
&nbsp;•&nbsp; 'Between the devil and the deep blue sea' (facing two equally bad choices) 
&nbsp;•&nbsp; 'Hit a brick wall' (encounter an obstacle)
</MultiLineNote>
</SubSection>

<SubSection>
#### Strategy 3: Trace the Origin Where Possible

As shown in the introduction, understanding where an idiom came from makes it significantly easier to remember. Many idioms originate in maritime life, military history, farming, sports, or classical literature. Spending a few minutes on etymology when you first encounter an idiom is a worthwhile investment.
</SubSection>

<SubSection>
#### Strategy 4: Keep a Dedicated Idiom Journal

A personal notebook dedicated to idioms serves a different function from a printed list. When you write an idiom by hand, alongside its meaning, origin (if known), and an original example sentence, you are encoding it across multiple memory systems at once. Review this journal for fifteen minutes every few days, not for hours at a stretch, but frequently and briefly.
</SubSection>

<SubSection>
#### Strategy 5: Practise Through Elimination

In examination conditions, you will rarely know every idiom. When you do not, work backwards: identify any option that is clearly literal (and therefore wrong), any option that has the wrong emotional tone, and any option that is logically inconsistent with the sentence context. This elimination approach can often reduce four options to one even with only partial knowledge.
</SubSection>

###  Four Exam Tips for Idioms

<SubSection>
#### Tip 1: Treat the Idiom as a Single Indivisible Unit

The most fundamental error students make with idioms is analysing them word by word. 'Burn the midnight oil' has nothing to do with fire or petroleum. The expression as a whole means to work or study late into the night. The moment you start breaking an idiom into components, you invite the literal interpretation trap. Commit to reading idioms as complete, irreducible units of meaning.
</SubSection>

<SubSection>
#### Tip 2: Read Every Word of the Surrounding Sentence

For Type 2 and Type 3 questions, the sentence provides the entire meaning context. If a sentence describes a situation of relief after a crisis, you can immediately filter out idioms that describe difficulty, deception, or indecision. Read the full sentence before evaluating any option.
</SubSection>

<SubSection>
#### Tip 3: Identify the Tone of the Idiom

Every idiom carries an emotional charge. Ask yourself: does this idiom describe something positive (success, courage, disclosure) or negative (failure, deception, avoidance)? This single question eliminates roughly half the options in most questions. For example, if the sentence describes someone overcoming a challenge triumphantly, idioms with negative or ambivalent tones are automatically wrong.
</SubSection>

<SubSection>
#### Tip 4: Eliminate Literal Interpretations First

Examiners routinely place a literal reading of the idiom's surface words as one of the four options. In 'get cold feet', option A was 'to feel physically cold'. Spot this trap immediately and eliminate it before you even begin evaluating the remaining three options.
</SubSection>
</Section>

<Section id="phrases">
##  Phrases and Phrasal Verbs

###  Definition: What Is a Phrase?

| **Term** | **Definition** |
| --- | --- |
| Phrase | A group of words that functions as a grammatical unit within a sentence but does not express a complete thought on its own. It lacks either a subject, a predicate, or both. |

A phrase gives meaning but not completeness. 'Across the rooftop', 'having worked all night', 'the exceptionally talented young surgeon' — each of these groups of words communicates something, but none of them can stand alone as a sentence. They must attach to other words to form a complete grammatical structure.

In examination contexts, however, the term 'phrase' is most often associated with phrasal verbs: combinations of a base verb and one or more particles (prepositions or adverbs) whose combined meaning differs from the verb alone. It is this specific type, the phrasal verb, that forms the core of phrase-based verbal ability questions.

###  How Phrasal Verb Questions Are Worded

| **Type** | **Instruction Wording** | **What Is Expected** |
| --- | --- | --- |
| Type 1 | 'Choose the phrasal verb that best replaces the highlighted part of the sentence.' | Identify which phrasal verb carries the same meaning as the underlined or highlighted expression. |
| Type 2 | 'Identify the phrasal verb that is closest in meaning to the given expression.' | Match the standalone expression to the correct phrasal verb. No supporting sentence is provided. |
| Type 3 | 'Choose the correct phrasal verb to complete the sentence.' | Select the phrasal verb that fits grammatically and meaningfully into the blank. |

<b>Click on the examples to open</b>

<details>
<summary><HighlightedText>Level 1: Foundational</HighlightedText></summary>

<b>Instruction:</b>Choose the phrasal verb that best replaces the highlighted part of the sentence.

The investigator was trying to <b>discover the truth</b> behind the disappearance.

A) find out &nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp; 
B) take over &nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp; 
C) wear out &nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp; 
D) break off

<b> Correct Answer: A) find out</b>

<MultiLineNote>
Explanation: 'Find out' means to discover or learn information, and it maps directly onto 'discover the truth'. 'Take over' means to assume control of something, which is unrelated. 'Wear out' means to exhaust or become unusable through repeated use. 'Break off' means to end or discontinue something abruptly. All three wrong options are common phrasal verbs that students sometimes confuse with each other, but none of them carries the sense of discovery.
</MultiLineNote>
</details>

<details>
<summary><HighlightedText>Level 2: Intermediate</HighlightedText></summary>

<b>Instruction:</b>Identify the phrasal verb that is closest in meaning to the given expression.

<b>TO COMPENSATE FOR</b>

A) bring about &nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp; 
B) make up for &nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp; 
C) set aside &nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp; 
D) come down with

<b> Correct Answer: B) make up for</b>

<MultiLineNote>
Explanation: 'Make up for' means to compensate for a loss, fault, or deficiency, which is an exact match. 'Bring about' means to cause something to happen. 'Set aside' means to reserve or save something for a specific purpose. 'Come down with' means to become ill with something. The trap here is 'bring about', which might sound vaguely constructive, but it describes causation, not compensation.
</MultiLineNote>
</details>

<details>
<summary><HighlightedText>Level 3: Advanced</HighlightedText></summary>

<b>Instruction:</b>Choose the correct phrasal verb to complete the sentence.

Despite months of careful planning, the proposed merger finally <b>-------</b>, leaving both companies in an uncertain position.

A) fell through &nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp; 
B) fell back &nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp; 
C) fell apart &nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp; 
D) fell behind

<b> Correct Answer: A) fell through</b>

<MultiLineNote>
Explanation: 'Fall through' means to fail to happen or be completed, especially after plans had been made, which is precisely what the sentence describes. 'Fall back' means to retreat or to resort to an alternative. 'Fall apart' means to break into pieces or to lose cohesion, which is slightly different: a merger that falls apart implies it disintegrated after starting, whereas one that falls through never reached completion. 'Fall behind' means to fail to keep up with a schedule or competitor. The distinction between 'fall through' and 'fall apart' is exactly the kind of fine semantic difference that Level 3 questions are designed to test.
</MultiLineNote>
</details>

###  Five Learning Strategies for Phrasal Verbs

<SubSection>
#### Strategy 1: Learn in Verb Families

Grouping phrasal verbs by their base verb is one of the most efficient study approaches available. The verb 'set', for example, generates: set out (begin a journey or task), set up (establish), set back (delay or hinder), set aside (reserve), and set off (begin a journey; also to trigger). Learning these as a family reveals patterns in how the particle modifies the base verb's meaning, which makes retention far more reliable than learning each expression in isolation.

| **Base Verb** | **Phrasal Verb** | **Meaning** |
| --- | --- | --- |
| come | come across | To encounter unexpectedly |
| come | come round | To regain consciousness; to change one's opinion |
| come | come up with | To produce an idea or solution |
| bring | bring about | To cause something to happen |
| bring | bring forward | To move to an earlier time or date |
| bring | bring up | To raise a topic; to raise a child |
</SubSection>

<SubSection>
#### Strategy 2: Learn Through Complete Sentences, Never in Isolation

A phrasal verb without a sentence context is almost meaningless. 'Come round' could be spatial (come round to the other side) or metaphorical (change one's opinion). Always attach a meaningful sentence to every phrasal verb you learn. The sentence demonstrates the grammatical pattern, including whether the verb is separable and whether it takes an object, as well as the meaning.
</SubSection>

<SubSection>
#### Strategy 3: Prioritise High-Frequency Verbs

A core set of verbs generates the majority of phrasal verbs that appear in examinations: get, make, take, put, bring, come, go, set, look, turn, run, break, give, hold, and fall. Mastering the phrasal verb families built around these fifteen verbs covers an enormous proportion of what is tested.
</SubSection>

<SubSection>
#### Strategy 4: Note Separability

Some phrasal verbs are separable, meaning an object can be placed between the verb and the particle ('turn the offer down' or 'turn down the offer'). Others are inseparable, meaning the object must follow the whole phrasal verb ('come across an interesting book', never 'come an interesting book across'). This distinction matters for grammatical accuracy, and some questions test it indirectly through contextual fit.
</SubSection>

<SubSection>
#### Strategy 5: Revise Through Quizzing, Not Rereading

Rereading a list of phrasal verbs produces only the illusion of learning. Active recall, where you cover the meaning and try to produce it from the verb alone, dramatically outperforms passive review. Test yourself daily on a rotating set of twenty phrasal verbs, spending no more than ten minutes at a time.
</SubSection>

### Four Exam Tips for Phrasal Verbs

<SubSection>
#### Tip 1: Read the Full Sentence Before Evaluating Options

The sentence context often carries the answer. If the sentence describes something that stopped or ended, phrasal verbs describing continuation are immediately eliminated. If the sentence describes something being discovered, phrasal verbs describing removal or postponement are wrong. Always extract the core action from the sentence before looking at the options.
</SubSection>

<SubSection>
#### Tip 2: Focus on the Particle, Not Just the Base Verb

The particle (up, down, off, through, out, over, back) determines the direction and quality of change the phrasal verb expresses. 'Up' often implies completion or improvement. 'Off' often implies disconnection or departure. 'Through' often implies completion of a process or penetration. Learning these general tendencies helps you reason about unfamiliar phrasal verbs.
</SubSection>

<SubSection>
#### Tip 3: Learn the Most Commonly Examined Phrasal Verbs

| **Phrasal Verb** | **Meaning** | **Commonly Confused With** |
| --- | --- | --- |
| carry out | To perform or complete a task | carry on (continue), carry over (transfer) |
| turn up | To arrive; to increase volume | turn down (reject; decrease volume) |
| put off | To postpone; to discourage | put on (dress; feign), put out (extinguish) |
| go through | To experience; to examine carefully | go over (review), go off (explode; go bad) |
| take on | To assume responsibility; to employ | take over (assume control), take up (begin a hobby) |
</SubSection>

<SubSection>
#### Tip 4: Eliminate by Meaning First, Then by Grammar

First remove any option whose meaning does not match the action described in the sentence. If two options remain with similar meanings, check which one is grammatically correct in the given sentence structure, whether it requires an object, whether it is separable, and whether the particle makes logical sense in context.
</SubSection>
</Section>

## One Word Substitutions

### Definition and Purpose

| **Term** | **Definition** |
| --- | --- |
| One Word Substitution | A single word that precisely replaces a longer descriptive phrase or group of words, preserving the full meaning of the original expression. |

Language is constantly looking for economies. A historian does not write 'a government in which power is held by a small number of people'; she writes 'oligarchy'. One word substitutions are the vocabulary of precision: single terms that capture complex ideas with no loss of meaning and considerable gain in efficiency.

What makes this topic fascinating, and challenging, is the precision required. Many options in examination questions describe something related but not exact. An 'extrovert' and a 'gregarious person' are similar, but they are not identical. A 'philanthropist' and an 'altruist' overlap considerably but differ in emphasis. Learning one word substitutions means learning not just the word but the exact definition it encodes.

###  Thematic Categories with Examples

The most effective way to study one word substitutions is by theme. The following categories account for the majority of OWS questions across competitive examinations.

| **Category** | **Expression** | **One Word Substitute** |
| --- | --- | --- |
| Persons and Professions | One who studies the structure and history of languages | Linguist |
| Persons and Professions | A person who pretends to have virtues or beliefs they do not possess | Hypocrite |
| Persons and Professions | One who manages the financial affairs of an institution | Treasurer |
| Places and Institutions | A place where wild animals are kept for public display | Zoological Park / Menagerie |
| Places and Institutions | A home for elderly people who need care | Geriatric home / Nursing home |
| Phobias and Manias | An extreme, irrational fear of confined spaces | Claustrophobia |
| Phobias and Manias | An obsessive passion for collecting and hoarding food | Sitomania |
| Speech and Language | A written declaration made under oath | Affidavit |
| Speech and Language | A speech delivered to an audience without prior preparation | Extempore |
| Behaviour and Character | A person who is indifferent to both pleasure and pain | Stoic |
| Behaviour and Character | The practice of having more than one spouse at a time | Polygamy |
| Science and Study | The scientific study of the development of living organisms | Ontogeny |
| Science and Study | The branch of medicine dealing with skin diseases | Dermatology |

### How OWS Questions Are Worded

| **Type** | **Instruction Wording** | **What Is Expected** |
| --- | --- | --- |
| Type 1 | 'Choose the one word that best replaces the given expression.' | Select the single word that most precisely captures the full meaning of the expression. |
| Type 2 | 'Identify the one-word substitute for the underlined phrase.' | The expression appears within a sentence; identify the single word it should be replaced with. |
| Type 3 | 'Select the correct one-word substitution for the given group of words.' | A descriptive phrase is given; select the precise term from the options. |

<summary><HighlightedText>Level 1: Foundational</HighlightedText></summary>

<b>Instruction:</b>Choose the one word that best replaces the given expression.

<b>Expression: A person who walks in their sleep</b>

A) Insomniac &nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp; B) Somnambulist &nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp; C) Narcoleptic &nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp; D) Noctambulist

<b> Correct Answer: B) Somnambulist</b>

<MultiLineNote>
Explanation: 'Somnambulist' precisely means a person who walks while asleep, from the Latin somnus (sleep) + ambulare (to walk). 'Insomniac' describes someone who is unable to sleep, which is the opposite of sleep-walking. 'Narcoleptic' describes a person with a neurological disorder causing sudden onset of sleep. 'Noctambulist' is a subtle trap: it also refers to someone who walks at night, but its connotation is broader and less clinically precise than somnambulist. ‘Noctambulist’ precisely means one who walks at night, which is not the same as one who walks while asleep. 
</MultiLineNote>
</details>

<details>
<summary><HighlightedText>Level 2: Intermediate</HighlightedText></summary>

<b>Instruction:</b>Identify the one-word substitute for the underlined phrase.

The elderly historian had spent his life in <b>the worship and reverent study of ancient traditions and customs</b>.

A) Iconoclasm &nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp; B) Antiquarianism &nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp; C) Nihilism &nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp; D) Fanaticism

<b> Correct Answer: B) Antiquarianism</b>

<MultiLineNote>
Explanation: 'Antiquarianism' is the devoted study of or interest in ancient things and customs. It fits the underlined phrase precisely. 'Iconoclasm' means the practice of attacking or criticising cherished beliefs and institutions, which is the opposite of reverent study. 'Nihilism' is the philosophical belief that life has no inherent meaning, and is entirely unrelated. 'Fanaticism' means extreme, uncritical devotion to a cause, which is too strong and too broad a term. It carries a negative connotation absent from the expression "worship and reverent study", which is described as something the historian spent his life doing positively. The three distractors all carry a sense of intensity about beliefs, making them plausible surface-level choices for students who read carelessly.
</MultiLineNote>
</details>

<details>
<summary><HighlightedText>Level 3: Advanced</HighlightedText></summary>

<b>Instruction:</b>Select the correct one-word substitution for the given group of words.

<b>Expression: An extreme, pathological desire to set things on fire</b>

A) Pyromania &nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp; B) Pyrophobia &nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp; C) Kleptomania &nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp; D) Ergomania

<b> Correct Answer: A) Pyromania</b>

<MultiLineNote>
Explanation: 'Pyromania' is a compulsive desire to start fires, from the Greek pyr (fire) + mania (obsession). 'Pyrophobia' is the irrational fear of fire. Note the suffix: -phobia means fear, while -mania means obsession. The suffix distinction is the core of this question. 'Kleptomania' is a compulsive urge to steal (klept- means to steal). 'Ergomania' is a compulsive need to work (ergo- means work). Knowing the roots pyr-, klepto-, ergo-, and the suffixes -mania and -phobia allows you to decode all four options systematically, even if you have never encountered them before.
</MultiLineNote>
</details>

###  Five Learning Strategies for One Word Substitutions

<SubSection>
#### Strategy 1: Organise by Theme, Not Alphabetically

An alphabetical list of OWS terms gives you no memory structure to hold them together. Thematic organisation, covering persons, places, phobias, manias, speech, and science, creates clusters that reinforce each other. When you revise the 'phobias' cluster, encountering 'claustrophobia' (fear of confined spaces) alongside 'agoraphobia' (fear of open spaces), 'acrophobia' (fear of heights), and 'xenophobia' (fear of strangers) makes each term easier to retain because of the shared structural pattern.
</SubSection>

<SubSection>
#### Strategy 2: Decode Using Roots, Prefixes, and Suffixes

A large proportion of one word substitutions are built from recognisable Latin and Greek components. Investing time in learning thirty core word parts unlocks hundreds of OWS terms at once. The table below illustrates this principle.

| **Root** | **Meaning** | **Example** | **Meaning** |
| --- | --- | --- | --- |
| anthrop- | Human | Philanthropist | A person who promotes the welfare of others |
| mis- | Hatred | Misanthrope | A person who dislikes other people |
| -logy | Study of | Ornithology | The study of birds |
| -phile | Lover of | Cinephile | A person who loves cinema |
| -cide | Killing | Fratricide | The killing of one's brother |
| omni- | All | Omniscient | Knowing everything |
</SubSection>

<SubSection>
#### Strategy 3: Learn the Full Sentence, Not Just the Swap

One word substitutions gain additional clarity from sentence context. 'Nepotism' is favouritism shown to relatives, but seeing it in a sentence such as 'The inquiry revealed widespread nepotism in the appointment of senior officials' makes the subtle nuance of power, favouritism, and institutional corruption vivid in a way that a dictionary definition alone cannot.
</SubSection>

<SubSection>
#### Strategy 4: Drill -mania and -phobia Terms Together

These two suffix families are among the most frequently tested in OWS sections. -Mania indicates obsession or compulsion; -phobia indicates irrational fear. Examiners routinely include both a -mania and a -phobia option for the same root in the answer choices. The student who confuses the suffix will choose the wrong one. Practise these in direct contrast pairs: pyromania vs. pyrophobia, agoraphobia vs. dromomania (compulsion to wander), and so on.
</SubSection>

<SubSection>
#### Strategy 5: Practise in Examination Format Regularly

Simply reading a list of OWS terms builds recognition, not recall. The examination asks you to produce the term from a description, which is the harder direction. Practise with flashcards (description on front, term on back) or timed OWS question sets. Aim for accuracy first, then speed.
</SubSection>

###  Four Exam Tips for One Word Substitutions

<SubSection>
#### Tip 1: Read the Entire Expression, Not Just the Keywords

The expression 'a person who studies insects' and 'a person who collects insects' are not the same. The one word substitute for the first is 'entomologist' (one who studies insects scientifically); for the second it might be 'insect collector'. One misread keyword can send you to the completely wrong option. Read every word of the expression before looking at the answer choices.
</SubSection>

<SubSection>
#### Tip 2: Identify the Category the Expression Belongs To

Before evaluating the options, ask yourself: what type of thing is the expression describing? Is it a person, a place, a condition, a fear, a type of government, a field of study? Identifying the category first allows you to immediately eliminate options that belong to a different category. If the expression clearly describes a mental condition, any option that names a person or a place is wrong.
</SubSection>

<SubSection>
#### Tip 3: Use Roots to Decode Unfamiliar Options

When you encounter an option you do not recognise, break it apart. If the answer choices for 'a compulsive desire to pull out one's own hair' include 'trichotillomania', you can reason: tricho- relates to hair (think 'trichology', the study of hair), till- relates to pulling, and -mania means obsession. The root system gives you the answer even without prior knowledge of the exact word.
</SubSection>

<SubSection>
#### Tip 4: Eliminate Close but Imprecise Options

This is the most important skill in OWS questions. Options are often carefully chosen to be nearly correct; they describe something related but not the exact thing. 'Vandal' (someone who wilfully damages property) is not the same as 'arsonist' (someone who deliberately sets fire to property). 'Altruist' (one who acts for the benefit of others) is not precisely the same as 'philanthropist' (one who promotes welfare through charitable acts, often with financial resources). Always demand the most precise match.
</SubSection>
</Section>

## Common Pitfalls Across All Three Topics

| **#** | **The Pitfall** | **Topic** | **The Fix** |
| --- | --- | --- | --- |
| 1 | Reading an idiom word by word and choosing the literal meaning | Idioms | Treat every idiom as a single, irreducible unit. Never break it apart. |
| 2 | Choosing a phrasal verb based on the base verb alone, ignoring the particle | Phrases | The particle changes everything. Always evaluate the full phrasal verb. |
| 3 | Selecting an OWS option that is related but too broad or too narrow | OWS | Demand exact precision. Related is not the same as correct. |
| 4 | Ignoring the sentence context in Type 2 and Type 3 questions | All three | Read the full sentence before evaluating any option. |
| 5 | Confusing -phobia (fear) and -mania (obsession) suffixes in OWS | OWS | Memorise these suffix meanings and practise them in contrast pairs. |
| 6 | Choosing an idiom option with the right general meaning but wrong tone | Idioms | Check tone: positive, negative, or neutral? Eliminate those that mismatch. |
