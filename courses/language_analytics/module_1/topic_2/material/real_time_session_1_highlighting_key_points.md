# Real Time Session 1: Highlighting Key Points

# Classroom Activity: “Highlighting Key Points in Professional Communication”

## Overview

This activity helps students develop the essential communication skill of identifying, organizing,
and emphasizing key points in professional English speaking. It trains learners to move beyond
information delivery and focus on clarity, prioritisation, and audience impact. Through structured
speaking tasks, students learn how to make their message concise, focused, and persuasive by
ensuring that the most important ideas stand out clearly. The activity enhances speaking skills,
critical thinking, structuring ability, and audience awareness.

# Stepping Stones

## 1) Identifying What Matters Most

Students learn to distinguish between:

- Core message (main idea/problem/solution).
- Supporting details (examples, explanations, data).
- Optional elaboration (additional context).

**On stage move:** Always ask—“What must the audience remember after this speech?”

## 2) Structuring for Clarity (Information Hierarchy)

Effective communication follows a clear priority order:

- Most important point first or clearly signposted.
- Supporting arguments in logical sequence.
- Minor details used only for reinforcement.

**On stage move:** Use clear transitions such as “First and foremost…”, “The key point is…”, “Most importantly…”

## 3) Emphasis through Language and Delivery

Key points are strengthened through:

- Repetition of critical ideas (without redundancy).
- Tone variation and pauses for emphasis.
- Signposting phrases to guide attention.
- Strategic slowing down during important statements.

**On stage move:** Slow speech + pause = signal importance.

## 4) Clarity through Simplification (ELM Approach)

Apply the Elaboration Likelihood Model:

- Central route audience: needs clear logic and evidence.
- Peripheral route audience: responds to confident delivery and clarity cues.

**On stage move:** Keep key points simple, direct, and easy to recall.

## 5) Reinforcing Key Ideas (Retention Techniques)

To ensure recall:

- Summarise key points at intervals.
- Use repetition in conclusion.
- Link each supporting idea back to the main message.

**On stage move:** End by restating the 2–3 most important takeaways.

# Divide the Class into Teams

## Speaking Teams

Students work in groups of 3–4.

Each group prepares a short presentation on a given topic (education, environment,
technology, social issues, etc.).

The focus is not on the quantity of ideas, but on highlighting 2–3 strongest points effectively.

## Audience Panel (Instructor + Students)

The panel listens and evaluates how clearly key points are identified and emphasised.

They may ask questions to test clarity and recall of main ideas.

# Role Allocation (Within Teams)

- **Opening Speaker** – Introduces the topic and states the main idea clearly.
- **Key Point Speaker** – Presents the 2–3 most important arguments.
- **Support Speaker** – Adds evidence/examples.
- **Closing Speaker** – Summarises and reinforces key points.

# Rules

- Each presentation must focus on clarity, not volume of content.
- Only essential points should be highlighted.
- All members must participate.
- Use formal and structured language.
- Avoid memorizing long scripts; focus on natural delivery.

# Time Limit

- Preparation Time: 5–7 minutes.
- Presentation Time: 4–5 minutes per team.
- Q&A: 1–2 minutes.
- Feedback: 1 minute.

# Evaluation Criteria

Teams will be assessed on:

- Ability to identify and prioritise key points.
- Clarity and structure of communication.
- Effective use of emphasis techniques.
- Logical flow of ideas.
- Audience engagement and recall impact.
- Confidence and delivery style.

# Example Play

## Topic: Reducing Screen Time Among Students.

### Opening Speaker:

Good morning everyone. Today we are presenting on a growing concern among
students—excessive screen time. Our main focus is on why it matters and how it can be
reduced effectively.

### Key Point Speaker:

The first key point is that excessive screen time directly affects concentration and academic
performance. Studies show that long hours on digital devices reduce attention span and
memory retention.

The second key point is its impact on health. Too much screen exposure leads to eye strain,
poor posture, and sleep disturbances, especially among teenagers.

The third key point is that small lifestyle changes can significantly reduce screen dependency,
such as setting daily usage limits and introducing screen-free study hours.

### Support Speaker:

For example, schools that implemented “digital detox hours” reported improved student focus
and participation in classroom activities. This shows that structured limits can create measurable
improvements.

### Closing Speaker:

To conclude, the most important points are: screen time affects learning, it impacts health, and
it can be controlled through simple habits. If we take small steps today, we can build healthier
and more focused learning environments tomorrow.

# Conclusion

This activity strengthens a critical professional communication skill—highlighting key points
effectively. It enables students to communicate with precision, structure, and clarity, ensuring
that their message is not only heard but remembered. By practising selective emphasis and
structured delivery, learners develop the ability to present ideas in a focused and impactful
manner in academic and professional contexts.
