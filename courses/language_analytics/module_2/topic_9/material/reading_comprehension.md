## What Reading Comprehension Really Tests

Reading comprehension is the most fundamental of all verbal ability skills. Every other question type — synonyms, fill in the blanks, analogies — tests isolated fragments of language ability. Reading comprehension tests the whole: your ability to read a connected piece of writing, understand what it says and what it implies, evaluate how the writer argues, and extract specific information accurately under time pressure.

The ability to read well is not the same as the ability to read fast. Many students who read widely still underperform on comprehension questions because they have not developed deliberate reading strategies. They read to absorb content, not to answer questions. This material will help you <HighlightedText>read purposefully</HighlightedText>.

<MultiLineNote>

<b>What Reading Comprehension Questions Assess</b><br><br>
<HighlightedText>Direct understanding</HighlightedText> — Did you grasp what the passage explicitly states?<br>
<br><HighlightedText>Inference</HighlightedText> — Can you deduce what the passage implies but does not state directly?<br>
<br><HighlightedText>Vocabulary in context</HighlightedText> — Can you determine what a word means as it is used in this specific passage?<br>
<br><HighlightedText>Main idea and purpose</HighlightedText> — Can you identify the central argument and the writer's intent?<br>
<br><HighlightedText>Tone and attitude</HighlightedText> — Can you read the emotional register and the writer's stance on the subject?<br>
<br><HighlightedText>Logical structure</HighlightedText> — Can you identify how the argument is organised and what supports the main claim?
</MultiLineNote>

In placement examinations, reading comprehension typically accounts for a significant portion of the verbal section. Passages range from 150 to 600 words. Each passage is followed by three to seven questions. The questions test different skills in a deliberately mixed sequence.

<Section id="anatomy">
## The Anatomy of a Reading Comprehension Question

### How Passages Are Presented

A reading comprehension set always consists of two parts: the passage and the questions. Understanding the structure of each helps you process both more efficiently.

| **Component** | **What It Contains** | **What to Do With It** |
| --- | --- | --- |
| The Passage | A connected piece of writing, typically 150 to 600 words. It may be expository, argumentative, descriptive, or narrative in nature. | Read once for overall understanding before attempting any question. Note tone, main idea, and argument structure. |
| The Questions | Three to seven questions, each with four options. Questions are typically not in the same order as the information in the passage. | Read each question carefully before returning to the passage. Know what you are looking for before you look. |
| The Options | Four choices per question. In well-designed questions, at least two options are plausible. The correct one will be precisely supported by the passage. | Never select an option based on what you know from outside the passage. Select only based on what the passage states or implies. |

### The Six Question Types —  Explained

Every reading comprehension question belongs to one of six types. <HighlightedText>Identifying the type instantly before answering saves time</HighlightedText> and prevents the most common errors.

<SubSection>
#### Type A — Direct / Factual Questions

These questions ask about information that is explicitly stated in the passage. The answer is in the passage; your job is to locate it accurately and match it to one of the four options. The trap is that the correct option usually <HighlightedText>paraphrases the passage rather than quoting it word for word</HighlightedText>. Options that copy the passage's exact phrasing are sometimes wrong because they are taken out of context.

<MultiLineNote>
<b>How to Recognise Direct Questions</b><br><br>
Key phrases: 'According to the passage...', 'The author states that...', 'Which of the following is mentioned in the passage?', 'The passage says that...'
</MultiLineNote>
</SubSection>

<SubSection>
#### Type B — Inference Questions

These questions ask about something the passage implies but never explicitly states. The answer must be logically deducible from what the passage says; it is not stated outright, but it follows necessarily from the stated information. The trap is that students often choose options that go too far beyond the passage, adding information the author never hinted at.

<MultiLineNote>
<b>How to Recognise Inference Questions</b><br><br>
Key phrases: 'It can be inferred from the passage that...', 'The passage suggests that...', 'Which of the following can be concluded from the passage?', 'The author implies that...'<br><br>
<HighlightedText>The golden rule for inference:</HighlightedText> the correct answer must be supportable from within the passage. If you cannot point to a specific sentence or set of sentences in the passage that justifies the inference, the option is wrong, even if it seems logical from general knowledge.
</MultiLineNote>
</SubSection>

<SubSection>
#### Type C — Main Idea / Title Questions

These questions ask you to identify the central argument or the most appropriate title for the passage. The correct answer captures the entire passage; not just the first paragraph, not just one interesting point. A common trap is an option that is true and mentioned in the passage but describes only a part of it, not the whole.

<MultiLineNote>
<b>How to Recognise Main Idea Questions</b><br><br>
Key phrases: 'The main idea of the passage is...', 'The passage is primarily about...', 'Which of the following best describes the passage?', 'The best title for this passage would be...'<br><br>
<HighlightedText>Strategy:</HighlightedText> The main idea is the one statement that, if removed, would make the entire passage fall apart. Supporting details, examples, and illustrations are not the main idea; they exist to support it.
</MultiLineNote>
</SubSection>

<SubSection>
#### Type D — Tone / Attitude Questions

These questions ask about the writer's emotional register: how they feel about the subject. The tone of a passage might be objective and analytical, enthusiastic and celebratory, critical and sceptical, cautious and tentative, ironic or satirical, or melancholy and reflective. Identifying tone requires reading between the lines: the words the author chooses, the examples they select, and the conclusions they draw all signal attitude.

<MultiLineNote>
<b>How to Recognise Tone Questions</b><br><br>
Key phrases: 'The author's tone in this passage can best be described as...', 'The author's attitude toward X is...', 'Which word best describes how the author views...', 'The author writes in a tone that is...'
</MultiLineNote>

| **Tone Word** | **What It Means** | **Clues in the Passage** |
| --- | --- | --- |
| Objective | Presenting facts without personal bias or emotion | Neutral language, balanced presentation of multiple views, no evaluative adjectives |
| Sceptical | Doubting or questioning the validity of something | Words like 'allegedly', 'claimed', 'purported'; rhetorical questions; counter-evidence |
| Laudatory | Expressing high praise or admiration | Strongly positive adjectives; enthusiasm; celebration of achievements |
| Sombre | Serious, thoughtful, and somewhat sad or grave | Measured pace; acknowledgement of difficulty; absence of optimism |
| Ironic | Saying one thing while meaning the opposite | Apparent praise that is actually criticism; exaggeration for comic effect |
| Cautious | Careful and restrained; not making strong claims | Hedging language: 'may', 'might', 'could', 'appears to'; qualifications |
</SubSection>

<SubSection>
#### Type E — Vocabulary in Context Questions

These questions highlight a word from the passage and ask what it means as used in that specific sentence. The challenge is that the word usually has multiple meanings, and only the passage context determines which one applies. <HighlightedText>Never choose the word's most common dictionary meaning</HighlightedText> without first reading the sentence it appears in.

<MultiLineNote>
<b>How to Recognise Vocabulary in Context Questions</b><br><br>
Key phrases: 'As used in paragraph 2, the word "X" most nearly means...', 'In line 14, "X" can best be replaced by...', 'The word "X" in the passage refers to...'
</MultiLineNote>
</SubSection>

<SubSection>
#### Type F — Author's Purpose / Structure Questions

These questions ask why the author included a specific piece of information, or how the passage is organised. For purpose questions, you must identify the <HighlightedText>rhetorical function</HighlightedText> of a sentence or paragraph: is it providing an example, introducing a counterargument, conceding a point, or reinforcing the main claim? For structure questions, you must describe the overall logical architecture of the passage.

<MultiLineNote>
<b>How to Recognise Purpose / Structure Questions</b><br><br>
Key phrases: 'The author mentions X in order to...', 'The purpose of the second paragraph is...', 'Which of the following best describes the organisation of the passage?', 'The example of X is used to...'
</MultiLineNote>
</SubSection>

</Section>

At this level, the passage uses accessible vocabulary and a straightforward structure. The argument moves in a clear, predictable direction. Questions test direct recall, basic inference, and simple tone identification.

<b>Passage A — Urban Forests and City Wellbeing</b>

*Cities around the world are increasingly investing in urban forests — networks of trees and green spaces woven into the built environment. The motivation behind this investment is not purely aesthetic. Research consistently shows that access to green spaces within cities reduces psychological stress, lowers blood pressure, and improves concentration among residents. Children who attend schools near parks and tree-lined streets demonstrate higher levels of sustained attention than those in purely built environments.*

*Beyond individual wellbeing, urban trees perform critical environmental services. A single mature tree can absorb up to 22 kilograms of carbon dioxide annually, while simultaneously releasing oxygen, filtering airborne particulates, and reducing ambient temperature through a process called transpiration. In cities where paved surfaces dominate, temperatures can be several degrees higher than in surrounding rural areas — a phenomenon known as the urban heat island effect. Trees interrupt this effect by providing shade and releasing moisture into the air.*

*Despite these benefits, urban forests face significant pressures. Development projects frequently remove mature trees that took decades to establish. Maintenance is expensive, and in tight municipal budgets, green infrastructure often loses out to road repairs or drainage improvements. Advocates argue that the economic case for urban trees is underappreciated: healthier populations place lower demands on healthcare systems, and properties near parks consistently command higher values in the real estate market.*

<b>Question A — Direct / Factual</b>

According to the passage, which of the following is a benefit of urban trees mentioned in the first paragraph?

A) They increase property values near parks.<br>
B) They help children concentrate better in school.<br>
C) They reduce carbon dioxide emissions from vehicles.<br>
D) They lower municipal maintenance costs.

<b> Answer: B) They help children concentrate better in school.</b>

<MultiLineNote>
Explanation: The first paragraph explicitly states that 'children who attend schools near parks and tree-lined streets demonstrate higher levels of sustained attention'. Options A and D are mentioned in the third paragraph, not the first. Option C is not stated anywhere in the passage; the passage mentions trees absorbing CO2 but not specifically from vehicles.
</MultiLineNote>

The word 'transpiration' as used in the second paragraph most nearly means:

A) A process by which plants release moisture into the air.<br>
B) A method of absorbing carbon dioxide from the atmosphere.<br>
C) A measurement of temperature difference between urban and rural areas.<br>
D) A phenomenon caused by paved surfaces in cities.

<b> Answer: A) A process by which plants release moisture into the air.</b>

<MultiLineNote>
Explanation: The passage states trees reduce ambient temperature 'through a process called transpiration'. The sentence also says trees provide shade and release moisture; the word 'while' links transpiration to releasing moisture. The context makes option A clear. Option B describes CO2 absorption, which the passage describes separately. Options C and D describe the urban heat island effect, not transpiration.
</MultiLineNote>

<b>Question C — Main Idea</b>

The main idea of the passage is best expressed as:

A) Urban forests are expensive to maintain and frequently removed during development.<br>
B) Trees in cities provide meaningful benefits to individuals and the environment but face serious challenges.<br>
C) Children benefit more from urban greenery than adults do.<br>
D) The urban heat island effect is the primary problem facing modern cities.

<b> Answer: B) Trees in cities provide meaningful benefits to individuals and the environment but face serious challenges.</b>

<MultiLineNote>
Explanation: Option B captures all three paragraphs: first paragraph (individual wellbeing), second paragraph (environmental services), and third paragraph (challenges). Option A captures only the problem side. Option C overstates a single detail from the first paragraph. Option D describes one phenomenon from the second paragraph, not the whole passage.
</MultiLineNote>

<b>Question D — Tone / Attitude</b>

The author's tone in this passage can best be described as:

A) Enthusiastically celebratory of urban green spaces.<br>
B) Pessimistic about the future of trees in cities.<br>
C) Informative and broadly supportive, while acknowledging difficulties.<br>
D) Detached and purely scientific.

<b> Answer: C) Informative and broadly supportive, while acknowledging difficulties.</b>

<MultiLineNote>
Explanation: The author presents the benefits of urban trees clearly and positively ('research consistently shows', 'critical environmental services'), while also acknowledging practical pressures such as development costs and municipal budget constraints. This balanced, evidence-led approach makes the tone informative and broadly supportive. It is not uncritically celebratory (A), since the passage acknowledges limitations and trade-offs; not pessimistic (B), because the overall emphasis remains on the value of urban forests; and not purely detached or scientific (D), because the passage consistently frames urban trees as socially and environmentally beneficial rather than discussing them in a fully neutral manner

At this level, the passage uses more sophisticated vocabulary and a more complex argumentative structure. Questions require genuine inference — reading between the lines — and more careful attention to the author's rhetorical choices.

<b>Passage B — Cognitive Bias and Decision-Making</b>

*Human beings like to believe they are rational actors. The persistence of this belief is itself a fascinating psychological phenomenon, because the evidence against it is overwhelming. Decades of research in cognitive psychology have established that our decision-making is systematically shaped by predictable patterns of error — mental shortcuts, known as heuristics, that serve us well in routine situations but lead us badly astray in complex or unfamiliar ones.*

*Among the most consequential of these biases is confirmation bias — the tendency to seek, interpret, and remember information in a way that confirms what we already believe. A financial analyst who believes a particular stock will rise will unconsciously weigh favourable news more heavily and dismiss unfavourable signals as anomalies. This is not dishonesty; it is an automatic feature of how the brain processes information. The analyst genuinely believes they are being objective.*

*What makes cognitive biases particularly resistant to correction is that awareness of them does not reliably prevent them. Studies have repeatedly shown that people who score highly on tests of rational thinking are not significantly less susceptible to confirmation bias than people who score poorly. The bias operates below the level of conscious deliberation. This presents a profound challenge for institutions — such as courts, hospitals, and financial regulators — that depend on the quality of individual human judgement.*

*The most promising responses to this challenge are structural rather than educational. Checklists, blind review processes, and mandatory consideration of alternative hypotheses do not eliminate bias — but they create systems that compensate for it. The evidence suggests that improving the environment in which decisions are made is more effective than trying to improve the decision-maker.*

<b>Instruction:</b>Read the passage carefully, then answer all four questions. Pay particular attention to what the passage states versus what it merely implies.

<b>Question A — Inference</b>

It can be inferred from the passage that the author views human rationality as:

A) A capacity that improves with formal education and training.<br>
B) Largely a reassuring myth that contradicts substantial psychological evidence.<br>
C) A skill that varies significantly between individuals.<br>
D) An ability that is reliable in routine situations but unnecessary in complex ones.

<b> Answer: B) Largely a reassuring myth that contradicts substantial psychological evidence.</b>

<MultiLineNote>
Explanation: The passage opens by calling the belief in human rationality 'a fascinating psychological phenomenon' and immediately states 'the evidence against it is overwhelming'. The entire passage argues that systematic biases undermine rational decision-making. Option B captures this inference. Option A is contradicted by the third paragraph, which says high scores on rational thinking tests do not reduce bias. Option C is not supported; the passage treats bias as universal, not variable between individuals. Option D misreads the first paragraph, which says heuristics 'serve us well in routine situations' but does not suggest rationality is 'unnecessary' in complex ones.
</MultiLineNote>

---

<b>Question B — Author's Purpose / Structure</b>

The author mentions the financial analyst in the second paragraph in order to:

A) Argue that financial professionals are more prone to bias than other decision-makers.<br>
B) Provide a concrete, relatable illustration of how confirmation bias operates in practice.<br>
C) Suggest that dishonesty is common in the financial sector.<br>
D) Contrast confirmation bias with other types of cognitive error.

<b> Answer: B) Provide a concrete, relatable illustration of how confirmation bias operates in practice.</b>

<MultiLineNote>
Explanation: The analyst example appears immediately after the definition of confirmation bias. Its function is illustrative; it makes the abstract concept concrete and recognisable. Option A is not supported; the author does not claim financial professionals are more biased. Option C is explicitly contradicted: 'this is not dishonesty'. Option D is not what the example does; no other bias is introduced in this paragraph.
</MultiLineNote>

<b>Question C — Inference</b>

Which of the following best describes the author's view of educational approaches to reducing cognitive bias?

A) They are the single most effective method available.<br>
B) They are equally effective as structural reforms in reducing cognitive bias.<br>
C) They are largely insufficient on their own.<br>
D) They have not yet been studied with sufficient rigour.

<b> Answer: C) They are largely insufficient on their own.</b>

<MultiLineNote>
Explanation: The fourth paragraph states that 'the most promising responses to this challenge are structural rather than educational' and that 'improving the environment in which decisions are made is more effective than trying to improve the decision-maker'. This clearly suggests that educational approaches alone are insufficient for significantly reducing cognitive bias. Option A directly contradicts the passage because the author explicitly favours structural interventions. Option B is incorrect because the passage states that structural approaches are more effective than educational ones, not equally effective. Option D is unsupported because the passage does not discuss limitations in research or lack of evidence.
</MultiLineNote>

<b>Question D — Tone / Attitude</b>

The tone of the passage is best described as:

A) Alarmist — warning readers of an imminent crisis in human judgement.<br>
B) Analytical and measured — presenting evidence and drawing reasoned conclusions.<br>
C) Optimistic — suggesting that cognitive bias can be effectively overcome.<br>
D) Dismissive — implying that efforts to improve decision-making are futile.

<b> Answer: B) Analytical and measured — presenting evidence and drawing reasoned conclusions.</b>

<MultiLineNote>
Explanation: The passage consistently cites research ('decades of research', 'studies have repeatedly shown'), presents specific examples, and draws qualified conclusions ('most promising responses'). This is the hallmark of an analytical, measured tone. It is not alarmist (no urgency or crisis framing), not optimistic ('structural tools do not eliminate bias', only compensate for it), and not dismissive (the final paragraph does propose productive responses).
</MultiLineNote>

<summary><HighlightedText>Level C — Advanced</HighlightedText></summary>

At this level, the passage is argumentatively complex, the vocabulary is sophisticated, and the questions require multi-step reasoning. The writer's position must be inferred from subtle cues, and some questions require evaluating the logical relationship between different parts of the argument.

<b>Passage C — The Philosophy of Memory</b>

*Memory is routinely spoken of as though it were an archive — a fixed repository of past experiences that can be retrieved, intact, whenever the retrieval conditions are right. This metaphor is intuitive, culturally entrenched, and almost certainly wrong. Contemporary cognitive science suggests that memory is better understood as a reconstructive process: each time we remember, we do not simply play back a recording but actively rebuild an experience from fragments, expectations, and present context.*

*The implications of this view are unsettling. If memory is reconstructive, then every act of remembering also involves the risk of distortion. The fragments from which a memory is assembled are themselves shaped by subsequent experiences, emotional states, and — perhaps most troublingly — by the very process of having previously remembered. Elizabeth Loftus's landmark research on eyewitness testimony demonstrated that witnesses who are asked leading questions during an interview later 'remember' details that never occurred. The memory was altered by the question, not by the original event.*

*Critics of this view sometimes argue that the reconstructive account of memory, taken to its logical conclusion, would render all testimony unreliable and all autobiography suspect. This objection mistakes the scope of the claim. Asserting that memory is fallible and subject to distortion is not the same as asserting that memory is worthless. A map that is imperfect is still a useful guide — the question is how imperfect, in what ways, and under what conditions the imperfections are most likely to emerge.*

*What the reconstructive view demands is not scepticism about memory in general, but calibrated judgement about which memories, recalled under which conditions, are most likely to be reliable. Memories formed under high emotional stress, recalled repeatedly over time, or shaped by social reinforcement are more susceptible to distortion than memories formed calmly, recalled promptly, and unsolicited by external cues. Knowing this does not invalidate memory — it provides a framework for evaluating it.*

---

<b>Question A — Vocabulary in Context</b>

The author uses the phrase 'almost certainly wrong' in the first paragraph to convey:

A) Personal uncertainty about the scientific consensus on memory.<br>
B) Confident rejection of the archive metaphor while maintaining appropriate epistemic caution.<br>
C) A concession that the archive metaphor has some validity.<br>
D) A lack of evidence for the reconstructive view of memory.

<b> Answer: B) Confident rejection of the archive metaphor while maintaining appropriate epistemic caution.</b>

<MultiLineNote>
Explanation: The phrase combines confidence ('certainly') with measured qualification ('almost'). In academic and analytical writing, authors often avoid absolute certainty even when they strongly support a position. This kind of hedging reflects intellectual precision, not personal doubt. Therefore, Option A is incorrect because the author is not uncertain about the scientific consensus; the overall tone of the passage is confident and explanatory. Option C is also incorrect because the author does not partially defend the archive metaphor; the following sentences clearly favour the reconstructive view of memory. Option D is incorrect because the passage immediately supports the reconstructive account with research evidence, particularly Loftus's studies on eyewitness testimony. Thus, the phrase conveys strong rejection expressed with careful academic restraint.

</MultiLineNote>

In the third paragraph, the author's reference to 'a map that is imperfect' serves to:

A) Contradict the critics' objection by showing that memory still has value despite its fallibility.<br>
B) Support the critics' view that reconstructive memory cannot be trusted.<br>
C) Introduce a new metaphor to replace the archive metaphor from the first paragraph.<br>
D) Suggest that all human knowledge is approximate and therefore unreliable.

<b> Answer: A) Contradict the critics' objection by showing that memory still has value despite its fallibility.</b>

<MultiLineNote>
Explanation: The map analogy appears in direct response to critics who argue the reconstructive view 'would render all testimony unreliable'. The author's counter-argument is: imperfect does not mean worthless. The map is imperfect but still useful; memory is fallible but still valuable. Option B gets the direction wrong. Option C is partially true (it is a new metaphor) but misses the argumentative function, which is to rebut the critics. Option D extends the analogy far beyond what the passage supports.
</MultiLineNote>

According to the passage, which of the following types of memory is most likely to be reliable?

<b> Answer: B) A memory of a calm experience, recalled soon after it occurred, without external prompting.</b>

<MultiLineNote>
Explanation: The final paragraph explicitly states the conditions under which memories are least susceptible to distortion: 'memories formed calmly, recalled promptly, and unsolicited by external cues'. Option B matches all three conditions. Option A describes conditions of high distortion (high emotional stress, recalled repeatedly). Option C matches 'social reinforcement', a distorting factor. Option D matches 'external cues', also a distorting factor.

<b>Question D — Inference</b>

Which of the following statements would the author of this passage most likely agree with?

A) Because memory is reconstructive, eyewitness testimony should be excluded from legal proceedings.<br>
B) The reconstructive nature of memory means that personal accounts of events are interesting but ultimately unreliable.<br>
C) Understanding the conditions under which memory distorts can help us use memory evidence more responsibly.<br>
D) The archive metaphor, while imprecise, remains a more practically useful model of memory than the reconstructive view.

<b> Answer: C) Understanding the conditions under which memory distorts can help us use memory evidence more responsibly.</b>

<MultiLineNote>
Explanation: The final paragraph makes exactly this argument: 'knowing this does not invalidate memory — it provides a framework for evaluating it.' The author advocates calibrated judgement, not wholesale rejection or uncritical acceptance. Option A goes far beyond the passage; the author explicitly says the reconstructive view does not make memory 'worthless'. Option B also overstates the sceptical position the author rejects in the third paragraph. Option D directly contradicts the first paragraph, where the archive metaphor is called 'almost certainly wrong'.
</MultiLineNote>

The primary purpose of the third paragraph in the overall argument is to:

A) Introduce the research of Elizabeth Loftus as further evidence for the reconstructive view.<br>
B) Acknowledge and rebut the strongest objection to the reconstructive account of memory.<br>
C) Concede that the reconstructive view has limits and may not apply to all types of memory.<br>
D) Transition between the scientific evidence and the philosophical implications.

<Section id="how-to-read">
## How to Read a Passage Strategically

The way you read a passage determines how well you answer its questions. Most students read comprehension passages the way they read anything else — for content, immersively, following the flow of the argument. This is the wrong mode for examination reading. You need to read <HighlightedText>actively and analytically</HighlightedText>, constantly asking why, how, and what.

<MultiLineNote>
<b>The Four-Step Active Reading Protocol</b><br><br>
<HighlightedText>Step A — First Sentence of Every Paragraph:</HighlightedText> Read only the first sentence of each paragraph before reading the full passage. First sentences almost always state the paragraph's main claim. This gives you a structural map of the passage before you read the details.<br><br>
<HighlightedText>Step B — Full Read:</HighlightedText> Now read the whole passage at a steady pace. Do not stop at unfamiliar words; keep reading. The surrounding sentences almost always make the meaning clear enough. As you read, mark (mentally or with underlining): the main claim, any contrast signals ('however', 'but', 'despite'), and the tone.<br><br>
<HighlightedText>Step C — Identify Tone and Structure:</HighlightedText> Before reading the questions, spend five seconds identifying the passage's tone (positive / critical / neutral / cautious) and its structure (does it present one argument? Two sides? A problem and solution?). This primes you to answer tone and structure questions without re-reading.<br><br>
<HighlightedText>Step D — Question-First Approach:</HighlightedText> Read each question and identify its type before returning to the passage. For direct questions, go straight to the relevant section. For inference questions, find the supporting evidence first. For vocabulary questions, read the full sentence containing the word. Never answer from memory alone; always verify in the passage.
</MultiLineNote>

### What to Mark While Reading

| **Mark This** | **Why It Matters** |
| --- | --- |
| <HighlightedText>The topic sentence of each paragraph (usually the first)</HighlightedText> | It states the paragraph's claim; all other sentences support it. |
| <HighlightedText>Contrast signals: however, but, despite, although, yet, on the other hand</HighlightedText> | These signal that the argument is about to shift direction, often where inference questions are set. |
| <HighlightedText>Cause-effect signals: because, therefore, as a result, consequently</HighlightedText> | These show logical relationships, frequently tested in inference and purpose questions. |
| <HighlightedText>Evaluative language: clearly, unfortunately, remarkably, merely</HighlightedText> | These reveal the author's tone and attitude, essential for tone questions. |
| <HighlightedText>Any names, dates, statistics, or specific claims</HighlightedText> | These are the targets of direct factual questions; knowing where they are saves time. |
</Section>

<Section id="strategies">
## Learning Strategies

| **Strategy** | **What to Do** |
| --- | --- |
| <HighlightedText>Read Actively Every Day</HighlightedText> | Choose one editorial, essay, or quality article every day and read it with a pen in hand. After each paragraph, write one sentence summarising the paragraph's main claim in your own words. After the full piece, write two sentences: the main idea and the author's tone. This practice, done consistently, is more valuable than any number of timed practice tests done passively. |
| <HighlightedText>Practise Each Question Type in Isolation First</HighlightedText> | Before attempting full passages with mixed question types, practise each question type separately. Spend a session doing only inference questions. Then spend a session on only tone questions. Then main idea questions. This deliberate separation helps you recognise the type instantly and apply the correct analytical mode. |
| <HighlightedText>Build a Vocabulary of Signal Words</HighlightedText> | Compile a personal list of signal words in three categories: contrast signals (however, yet, despite, on the other hand), support signals (furthermore, in addition, because, since), and concession signals (although, even though, while it is true that). Recognising these instantly tells you whether the argument is moving forward, reversing, or qualifying. |
| <HighlightedText>Summarise Before Answering</HighlightedText> | After reading a passage, before looking at any question, think a one-sentence summary of the passage. This forces you to identify the main claim and prevents you from being confused by the details when you return to answer specific questions. Students who skip this step often confuse supporting details for the main idea. |
| <HighlightedText>Analyse Your Errors by Type</HighlightedText> | When reviewing practice tests, categorise every wrong answer by question type. Are you consistently missing inference questions? Tone questions? Vocabulary in context? Each type requires a different skill, and knowing which skill is weakest tells you exactly where to focus your preparation. |
</Section>

<Section id="exam-tips">
## Exam Tips

| **Tip** | **What to Do** |
| --- | --- |
| <HighlightedText>Read Questions Before the Passage (Selectively)</HighlightedText> | Skim the questions — not the options, just the questions — before reading the passage. This takes thirty seconds and tells you what to look for. If you know one question asks about the author's tone, you will notice tone-indicating language as you read. |
| <HighlightedText>Answer From the Passage, Not From Your Knowledge</HighlightedText> | This is the most violated rule in reading comprehension. The correct answer is always supported by the passage. If you find yourself thinking 'I know from general knowledge that this must be right', stop. Correct answers are directly traceable to a sentence or combination of sentences in the passage. |
| <HighlightedText>Eliminate Rather Than Select</HighlightedText> | For difficult questions, work through the options in elimination mode rather than selection mode. Find what is wrong with each option before committing to the best one. Most wrong options contain one of three errors: they state the opposite of what the passage says, they go beyond what the passage supports, or they are true of the passage but answer the wrong question. |
| <HighlightedText>For Inference Questions, Test Every Option Against the Passage</HighlightedText> | Inference questions have the highest error rate because students confuse 'plausible' with 'supported'. Test each option: can you point to a specific sentence or combination of sentences that makes this inference necessary? If not, even if the option seems reasonable, it is wrong. |
| <HighlightedText>For Tone Questions, Focus on Word Choice</HighlightedText> | Tone is carried almost entirely by the words the author chooses. Look for evaluative adjectives and adverbs ('merely', 'remarkably', 'unfortunately', 'clearly'), the selection of examples, and hedging language ('may', 'suggests', 'appears to'). The presence or absence of hedging language alone usually distinguishes objective from subjective tones. |
| <HighlightedText>Watch the Clock: One to Two Minutes Per Question</HighlightedText> | Reading comprehension is often the section that students run out of time on. Set a pace of approximately two minutes per question, including time for re-reading relevant sections. If a question is taking longer than two minutes, mark it, move on, and return if time permits. |
</Section>

<Section id="common-errors">
## Common Errors and How to Avoid Them

| **Error** | **Why It Happens** | **Prevention** |
| --- | --- | --- |
| <HighlightedText>Choosing an option that is true from general knowledge but not stated in the passage.</HighlightedText> | Students forget the rule: answer from the passage only. | Before selecting, ask: 'Where in the passage does this come from?' If you cannot answer, eliminate. |
| <HighlightedText>Selecting a supporting detail as the main idea.</HighlightedText> | The detail is interesting and mentioned in the passage, so it feels correct. | The main idea must account for the entire passage. If it leaves out major sections, it is a detail, not the main idea. |
| <HighlightedText>Choosing an extreme option for inference questions.</HighlightedText> | Extreme options (always, never, completely, entirely) often feel definitive and therefore correct. | Passages almost never support extreme claims. Qualified options ('typically', 'tends to', 'often') are usually closer to what passages actually imply. |
| <HighlightedText>Misidentifying tone because of a single positive or negative sentence.</HighlightedText> | Students find one evaluative sentence and label the whole tone from it. | Tone is the dominant register of the whole passage, not a single sentence. Re-read the opening and closing paragraphs if tone is unclear. |
| <HighlightedText>Answering vocabulary questions using the word's most common meaning.</HighlightedText> | The most common meaning is familiar and feels safe. | Always read the full sentence containing the word. The context almost always makes the contextual meaning clear and may differ significantly from the primary definition. |
| <HighlightedText>Spending too long on a difficult passage and running out of time.</HighlightedText> | Difficulty calibration under time pressure is an underpractised skill. | Set a maximum of two minutes per question. If stuck, make your best educated guess, mark the question, and move on. |
</Section>

<Section id="cheat-sheet">
## Quick-Reference Cheat Sheet

| **Question Type** | **How to Recognise It** | **Key Strategy** |
| --- | --- | --- |
| Direct / Factual | 'According to the passage...', 'The author states...' | Locate the section; match the paraphrase, not the exact words. |
| Inference | 'It can be inferred...', 'The passage suggests...' | Find supporting evidence in the passage; confirm the inference follows necessarily. |
| Main Idea | 'The main idea is...', 'The passage is primarily about...' | The answer must account for the whole passage, not just one part. |
| Tone / Attitude | 'The author's tone is...', 'The author views X as...' | Read word choices and evaluative language; identify dominant emotional register. |
| Vocabulary in Context | 'The word X most nearly means...' | Read the full sentence; substitute each option; choose the best semantic fit. |
| Purpose / Structure | 'The author mentions X in order to...', 'The purpose of the final paragraph is...' | Identify the rhetorical function: example, counterargument, reinforcement, concession. |
</Section>
