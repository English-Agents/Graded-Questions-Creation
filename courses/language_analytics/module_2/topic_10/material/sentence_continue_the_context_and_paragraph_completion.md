## Sentence Completion


A sentence completion question gives you a sentence with one or two blanks and four options. You choose the option that best fills the blank so the sentence is both grammatically correct and logically coherent.

This sounds straightforward, but the difficulty lies in the word **'best'**. Examiners almost always include at least one option that is grammatically acceptable but logically wrong in context. Choosing between them requires you to read the sentence as a complete unit, not just the words near the blank.

<MultiLineNote>

Every sentence completion question has a <HighlightedText>logical direction</HighlightedText> built into it. Your job is to find that direction before you read the options. Once you know whether the blank should be positive or negative, cause or effect, contrast or continuation, the correct option becomes much easier to identify.
</MultiLineNote>



### Reading Connective Logic

Connective words are the single most useful signal in any sentence completion question. They tell you the relationship between the blank and the rest of the sentence. <HighlightedText>Ignoring them is the most common reason students pick the wrong answer.</HighlightedText>

<SubSection>
**Contrast connectives: although, despite, however, yet, even though, while, but**

When you see a contrast connective, the blank must express something in the opposite direction to the rest of the sentence. If the sentence says a project succeeded despite something, that something must be an obstacle, not an advantage.
</SubSection>

<SubSection>
**Continuation connectives: therefore, consequently, as a result, furthermore, hence**

These tell you the blank follows logically from what came before. The blank should reinforce or extend the established idea, not contradict it.
</SubSection>

<SubSection>
**Cause connectives: because, since, as, owing to, due to**

These introduce a reason. The blank is either the cause or the effect. Identify which side you are on, then choose accordingly.
</SubSection>

<SubSection>
**Condition connectives: if, unless, provided that, only when**

The blank describes an outcome that depends on the stated condition. The two parts of the sentence must be logically compatible.
</SubSection>

### The Three Question Formats

<b>Click on the examples to open</b>

<details>
<summary><HighlightedText>Format 1: Single Blank</HighlightedText></summary>

One blank, four options. The sentence gives you enough context through the surrounding words and connectives to narrow the answer to one option. This is the most common format.

<b>Question:</b>The governor's speech was surprisingly <b>-------</b>, given that the situation called for firm and decisive language.

A) forthright &nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp; 
B) ambiguous &nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp; 
C) candid &nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp; 
D) resolute

<b> Answer: B) ambiguous</b>

<MultiLineNote>
Explanation: 'Given that the situation called for firm and decisive language' sets up an expectation. The word 'surprisingly' tells you the speech fell short of that expectation. So the blank must mean something like 'vague' or 'unclear'. Ambiguous fits exactly. Forthright, candid, and resolute all lean toward directness, which would not be surprising given the situation.
</MultiLineNote>
</details>

<details>
<summary><HighlightedText>Format 2: Double Blank</HighlightedText></summary>

Two blanks in the same sentence, with a paired option for each. Both words must work at the same time. If either word in a pair fails, the entire option is wrong. The fastest approach is to <HighlightedText>test one blank at a time</HighlightedText> across all four options and eliminate the pairs where the word clearly does not fit the sentence.

<b>Question:</b>The new director's style was so <b>-------</b> that even the most <b>-------</b> employees found themselves willing to collaborate.

A) abrasive, cooperative &nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp; 
B) inclusive, resistant<br>
C) rigid, enthusiastic &nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp; 
D) democratic, indifferent

<b> Answer: B) inclusive, resistant</b>

<MultiLineNote>
Explanation: The sentence describes a leadership style that successfully encouraged collaboration even among difficult employees. "Inclusive" fits the idea of a cooperative leadership approach, and "resistant" logically describes employees who would normally be unwilling to collaborate. Option A fails because an "abrasive" style would not typically encourage teamwork. Option C is inconsistent because a "rigid" style does not naturally make employees more collaborative. Option D is weaker because "indifferent" employees are unconcerned rather than actively unwilling, making the contrast less precise than "resistant."
</MultiLineNote>
</details>

<details>
<summary><HighlightedText>Format 3: Confusable Word Pairs</HighlightedText></summary>

These questions use pairs of words that are commonly mixed up: affect and effect, principal and principle, eminent and imminent, discreet and discrete, complement and compliment. The sentence provides enough context to identify which word is needed, but only if you read it carefully.

<b>Question:</b>The board decided to bring in an <b>-------</b> consultant rather than promote internally, citing the need for an unbiased perspective.

A) external &nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp; 
B) extraneous &nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp; 
C) exterior &nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp; 
D) extrinsic

<b> Answer: A) external</b>

<MultiLineNote>
Explanation: All four options derive from a Latin root meaning 'outside', which is why they tempt. External means coming from outside an organisation, which fits hiring a consultant. Extraneous means irrelevant or unnecessary. Exterior refers to an outside surface. Extrinsic means not inherent to something, often used in academic psychology. Only 'external consultant' is a natural collocation in a business context.
</MultiLineNote>
</details>
</SubSection>


## What Examiners Are Actually Testing
<SubSection>
**Vocabulary precision, not just vocabulary range**

Examiners rarely test whether you know a rare word. They test whether you know the precise difference between near-synonyms. 'Reticent' and 'taciturn' both describe quietness, but reticent implies reluctance to speak about something specific, while taciturn describes a general personality trait. A sentence will reveal which is needed.
</SubSection>
<SubSection>
**Collocation awareness**

Some words travel naturally with others. We say 'raise a question', not 'lift a question'. We say 'make a decision', not 'do a decision'. All four options in a question might mean roughly the same thing, but only one collocates correctly with the other words in the sentence. If an option sounds subtly odd next to the surrounding words, that is usually a reliable signal it is wrong.
</SubSection>
<SubSection>
**Grammar as an elimination tool**

Before thinking about meaning, ask what part of speech the blank requires. If the blank follows 'an' and precedes a noun, it must be an adjective. If it follows 'to' at the end of the sentence, it must be a base verb. This single question can eliminate one or two options immediately, without any vocabulary knowledge at all.
</SubSection>
<SubSection>
**Tone matching**

Every sentence has a register: formal, academic, conversational, emotional, neutral. The word that fills the blank must match this register. An informal word in a formal sentence feels wrong even if the meaning is approximately right. Academic sentences call for precise, measured words. Conversational sentences allow more casual choices.
</SubSection>

### Difficulty Levels with Worked out Examples

<b>Click on the examples to open</b>

<details>
<summary><HighlightedText>Level 1: Foundational</HighlightedText></summary>

The vocabulary is familiar, the connective is obvious, and the wrong options are clearly distinguishable. The primary skill tested is reading the full sentence and matching the blank's direction.

<b>Instruction:</b>Choose the appropriate word.

The medicine had been stored incorrectly and was no longer <b>-------</b> for human consumption.

A) suitable &nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp; 
B) exceptional &nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp; 
C) mandatory &nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp; 
D) profitable

<b> Answer: A) suitable</b>

<MultiLineNote>
Explanation: 'No longer suitable for human consumption' is a standard phrase in food and pharmaceutical contexts. The sentence describes a product that has gone bad. Suitable means appropriate or fit for a purpose, which is the logical word here. The others carry no meaning that relates to whether something is safe or appropriate to consume.
</MultiLineNote>
</details>

<details>
<summary><HighlightedText>Level 2: Intermediate</HighlightedText></summary>

The connective is less obvious, vocabulary is more specialised, and the wrong options are calibrated to mislead. Students must analyse the sentence's logic rather than relying on instinct.

<b>Instruction:</b>Choose the option that best completes the sentence.

The scientist's initial findings were <b>-------</b>, but further testing confirmed that her hypothesis was correct.

A) validated &nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp; 
B) inconclusive &nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp; 
C) celebrated &nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp; 
D) documented

<b> Answer: B) inconclusive</b>

<MultiLineNote>
Explanation: The word 'but' signals a contrast. Since the second half says the hypothesis was eventually confirmed, the first half must describe a stage where it was not yet confirmed. Inconclusive means not leading to a definite conclusion, which is precisely the contrast needed. Validated would mean the same as the second half, removing the contrast. Celebrated and documented do not logically contrast with confirmation.
</MultiLineNote>
</details>

<details>
<summary><HighlightedText>Level 3: Advanced</HighlightedText></summary>

Double blanks, complex tense logic, or subtle near-synonyms. Both words must work together. Students who can only evaluate one blank at a time will struggle here.

<b>Instruction:</b>Choose the pair that best completes the sentence.

The auditors <b>-------</b> that several transactions had <b>-------</b> compliance guidelines during the internal fraud investigation.

A) confirmed, violated &nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp; 
B) denied, followed
C) suggested, met &nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp; 
D) confirmed, followed

<b> Answer: A) confirmed, violated</b>

<MultiLineNote>
Explanation: The phrase "during the internal fraud investigation" creates a context of suspected misconduct. "Confirmed" establishes a definite audit conclusion, and "violated" fits logically with breaches of compliance guidelines. Option B contradicts the investigative tone because auditors who "denied" wrongdoing would not then imply compliance concerns. Option C is weaker because "suggested" is too tentative for the outcome of a formal investigation, and "met" implies compliance rather than violation. Option D is grammatically possible, but "confirmed that transactions followed guidelines" conflicts with the negative context established by the fraud investigation. 
</MultiLineNote>
</details>
</SubSection>

<SubSection>
### Strategy for Sentence Completion

| **Step** | **What to Do** |
| --- | --- |
| <HighlightedText>Cover the options. Read the sentence first.</HighlightedText> | Before you look at the options, read the sentence through completely and form a prediction for what kind of word the blank needs. Even a vague prediction, like 'something negative about the plan' or 'a verb in past tense', filters your thinking before the options try to distract you. |
| <HighlightedText>Find the connective and name its direction.</HighlightedText> | Mark any connective word and decide: is this a contrast, a continuation, or a cause? Once you know the direction, you know whether the blank should reinforce or oppose the rest of the sentence. This single step eliminates wrong options faster than any other. |
| <HighlightedText>Check grammar before meaning.</HighlightedText> | Ask what part of speech the blank requires. If an option is the wrong grammatical type, eliminate it immediately, regardless of its meaning. This takes three seconds and often removes one or two options right away. |
| <HighlightedText>Substitute and read the full sentence.</HighlightedText> | Do not just check whether a word 'fits the gap'. Substitute your chosen word into the sentence and read the whole sentence from beginning to end. If it sounds natural and logically coherent, you have the right answer. If something feels off, it usually means another option is better. |
| <HighlightedText>For double blanks, test the first word across all four options.</HighlightedText> | Evaluate the first blank independently. Eliminate any pair whose first word clearly fails. You will usually reduce four options to two. Then test the remaining pairs as complete units and choose the one where both words work together. |
</SubSection>


### Exam Tips for Sentence Completion

<SubSection>
**Do not decide from the first half of the sentence**

The decisive context clue is often in the second half, after the blank. Reading up to the blank and then guessing is one of the most common sources of error at all difficulty levels. Always read the complete sentence before evaluating options.
</SubSection>
<SubSection>
**Treat tone as a filter, not a detail**

If a sentence is describing a professional achievement, the blank must carry a positive word. If it is describing a failure or disappointment, the blank must carry a negative one. Any option that mismatches the sentence's emotional direction can be eliminated before you even think about precise meanings.
</SubSection>
<SubSection>
**Be suspicious of words that look similar to the correct answer**

Examiners often include a near-synonym as a distractor, precisely because it looks like it belongs. Words like 'notorious' and 'notable', 'infer' and 'imply', or 'affect' and 'effect' are placed together deliberately. Slow down when you see words that look like siblings of each other.
</SubSection>
<SubSection>
**Collocations break near-synonyms**

When two options have similar meanings, check which one collocates more naturally with the surrounding words. 'Strong argument' is natural. 'Powerful argument' is also fine. But 'strong evidence' is far more common than 'powerful evidence'. The option that sounds most natural in the company of the surrounding words is almost always correct.
</SubSection>
</Section>

<Section id="paragraph-completion">
## Paragraph Completion

A paragraph completion question gives you a paragraph with a blank **at the beginning, middle, or end**, and asks you to choose the sentence that fits that blank most naturally. The four options are all full sentences. 

The correct one must follow logically from the sentences before the blank, lead naturally into the sentences after it, maintain the paragraph's tone, and not introduce any idea that the paragraph never develops.


<MultiLineNote>
<b>Why Paragraph Completion Is Not the Same as Cloze or Fill-in-the-Blank</b><br><br>
Cloze and fill-in-the-blank questions ask for a word. Paragraph completion asks for a sentence. That shift in scale changes everything. You are no longer matching a word to a grammatical slot. You are matching an idea, a logical move, and a tone to the larger structure of a paragraph. The skills involved are <HighlightedText>closer to reading comprehension than to vocabulary testing.</HighlightedText>
</MultiLineNote>


<SubSection>
### Where the Blank Falls and What That Means

| **Blank Position** | **What the Correct Sentence Must Do** |
| --- | --- |
| <HighlightedText>Blank at the beginning (the opening sentence)</HighlightedText> | If the blank is the first sentence, it must introduce the paragraph's topic and set up everything that follows. Read the rest of the paragraph first, identify what the paragraph is about, and choose the sentence that best serves as a topic sentence. It should be broad enough to cover all the points the paragraph makes, but specific enough to actually name the subject. |
| <HighlightedText>Blank in the middle (a connecting sentence)</HighlightedText> | A blank in the middle of a paragraph must bridge the sentence before it and the sentence after it. Read the sentence immediately before the blank and the sentence immediately after it. The correct option must follow naturally from the first and lead cleanly into the second. Look for pronoun references, repeated vocabulary, and logical connectives as signals. |
| <HighlightedText>Blank at the end (the concluding sentence)</HighlightedText> | If the blank is the last sentence, it must conclude the paragraph. It should not introduce new information. It should bring the paragraph's argument to a logical landing point. Options that add new facts, raise new questions, or shift to a different aspect of the topic are almost always wrong for end-position blanks. |
</SubSection>

<SubSection>
### Five Signals That Identify the Correct Sentence

| **Signal** | **What to Look For** |
| --- | --- |
| <HighlightedText>Pronoun reference</HighlightedText> | If the sentence before the blank ends with a noun, and one of the options begins with 'it', 'they', 'this', or 'these', that pronoun likely refers to that noun. This is called a pronoun reference chain. When it is present and clear, it is a strong indicator that the option belongs in that position. Equally, an option that uses a pronoun with no clear referent in the surrounding text is almost certainly wrong. |
| <HighlightedText>Logical connectives within the option</HighlightedText> | Options that begin with 'However', 'Therefore', 'In contrast', or 'As a result' are signalling their logical relationship to what comes before them. 'However' means the option contrasts with the preceding sentence. 'Therefore' means it follows as a consequence. Match the connective in the option to the actual relationship between the sentences around the blank. |
| <HighlightedText>Repeated or developed vocabulary</HighlightedText> | Well-written paragraphs chain ideas across sentences by picking up key words from the previous sentence and developing them. If the preceding sentence mentions 'infrastructure funding', the correct option might refer to 'this investment' or 'such expenditures'. Look for which option picks up the thread from the sentence before the blank. |
| <HighlightedText>Tonal consistency</HighlightedText> | Every paragraph has a register: formal or casual, analytical or emotional, optimistic or cautious. The correct sentence should match the register of the surrounding text. An option that is dramatically more casual, more dramatic, or more technical than the rest of the paragraph is almost certainly wrong, even if its content seems related. |
| <HighlightedText>The 'new idea' test</HighlightedText> | Apply this particularly to options being considered for middle or end positions: does this option introduce an idea that the paragraph never develops? If yes, it is almost certainly wrong. A paragraph is a coherent unit, and every sentence in it should serve the paragraph's central purpose. An option that redirects or expands the scope of the paragraph is not the correct answer. |
</SubSection>

<SubSection>
### Full Worked Example: Three Blank Positions

The three questions below use the same base paragraph, with the blank placed in a different position each time. Working through all three exercises together is the most effective way to understand how a blank position changes the task.

<b>Base Paragraph:</b>

*[A] --------. Over the past decade, several major cities have introduced congestion pricing zones, where drivers pay a fee to enter dense urban areas during peak hours. The revenue generated is typically redirected towards public transport improvements. [B] --------. Critics, however, argue that such schemes disproportionately burden lower-income commuters who have no viable alternative to driving. [C] --------.*

<b>Click on the questions to open</b>

<details>
<summary><HighlightedText>Question A: Blank at the Beginning</HighlightedText></summary>

<b>Instruction:</b>Choose the sentence that best serves as the opening sentence of the paragraph. Read the full paragraph first, then evaluate each option against the paragraph's central topic and scope.

A) Public transport usage has declined in many cities over the past twenty years due to a rise in car ownership.<br>
B) Congestion pricing is one of several demand-management tools being adopted by city planners to reduce traffic and improve air quality.<br>
C) Urban pollution levels have been linked to a range of respiratory conditions in densely populated areas.<br>
D) Several economists have argued that road pricing is more efficient than fuel taxes as a means of managing transport demand.

<b> Answer: B)</b>

<MultiLineNote>
Explanation: The paragraph is about congestion pricing: what it is, how revenue is used, and the debate around it. The first sentence must introduce this topic broadly. Option B does exactly that. Option A introduces a different topic: declining public transport use. Option C focuses on urban pollution, which the paragraph does not address. Option D introduces a comparison with fuel taxes, which also never appears in the paragraph.
</MultiLineNote>
</details>

<details>
<summary><HighlightedText>Question B: Blank in the Middle</HighlightedText></summary>

<b>Instruction:</b>Choose the sentence that best connects the sentence before the blank and the sentence after it. The sentence before blank [B] says revenue is redirected to public transport improvements. The sentence after says critics argue the scheme burdens lower-income commuters.

A) Some cities have reported significant reductions in traffic volumes and measurable improvements in air quality following the introduction of these zones.<br>
B) Many transport economists believe that flat-rate congestion charges are inherently unfair to all users.<br>
C) The political opposition to congestion pricing has grown stronger in recent years.<br>
D) Urban planners frequently consult with environmental groups before introducing new transport policies.

<b> Answer: A)</b>

<MultiLineNote>
Explanation: The sentence before the blank describes a benefit (revenue for public transport). The sentence after introduces criticism. The correct middle sentence should complete the positive case for the scheme before the criticism begins, setting up a 'benefits, but also critics' structure. Option A describes measurable positive outcomes, which bridges naturally into the critical view that follows. Option B already introduces unfairness, which overlaps with the criticism that comes after. Options C and D introduce political opposition and planning consultations, neither of which connects to what comes before or after.
</MultiLineNote>
</details>

<details>
<summary><HighlightedText>Question C: Blank at the End</HighlightedText></summary>

<b>Instruction:</b>Choose the sentence that best concludes the paragraph. The paragraph has moved from describing what congestion pricing is, to its benefits, to the criticism that it burdens lower-income commuters. The final sentence should close this arc.

A) Whether congestion pricing can be made genuinely equitable depends on how the revenue is targeted and whether affordable transport alternatives are in place.<br>
B) Governments should consider abolishing all urban road charges and replacing them with public ownership of transport networks.<br>
C) Air quality data from cities with congestion zones may not accurately reflect conditions in surrounding suburban areas.<br>
D) The debate about transport funding models is likely to intensify as electric vehicles become more common.

<b> Answer: A)</b>

<MultiLineNote>
Explanation: The paragraph ends with a tension between benefits and criticism. Option A resolves that tension by pointing to the conditions under which the scheme could work for everyone, which is a natural and complete conclusion. Option B introduces a radical policy proposal not raised anywhere in the paragraph. Option C shifts to suburban air quality data, which is unrelated to the paragraph's focus. Option D introduces electric vehicles, a new topic that the paragraph never mentions.
</MultiLineNote>
</details>
</SubSection>

<SubSection>
### Strategy for Paragraph Completion

| **Step** | **What to Do** |
| --- | --- |
| <HighlightedText>Read the whole paragraph before evaluating any option.</HighlightedText> | Students who read only the sentences around the blank will keep falling for options that seem to fit locally but contradict something stated elsewhere in the paragraph. A complete read first takes thirty seconds and prevents the most common category of error. |
| <HighlightedText>Identify the paragraph's logical structure before placing the blank.</HighlightedText> | Most paragraphs follow a recognisable pattern: problem, then solution; or definition, then example, then implication; or claim, then evidence, then conclusion. Identifying the pattern tells you what role the blank is supposed to play in that structure. |
| <HighlightedText>For middle blanks, read the sentence before and the sentence after as a pair.</HighlightedText> | Write down or note the last idea in the preceding sentence and the first idea in the following sentence. The correct option must bridge these two anchors. Any option that creates a sudden jump from one to the other is wrong. |
| <HighlightedText>Apply the new idea test to every option.</HighlightedText> | Ask of each option: does this sentence introduce an idea that the paragraph never addresses again? If yes, eliminate it. Paragraphs do not drop new ideas and abandon them. Coherence is the rule. |
| <HighlightedText>For end blanks, reject options that open new threads.</HighlightedText> | Concluding sentences should close the paragraph, not expand it. Any option that introduces a new fact, raises a new question, or shifts to a new aspect of the topic is wrong for an end-position blank. The correct option should make the reader feel the paragraph is complete. |
</SubSection>

<SubSection>
### Exam Tips for Paragraph Completion

<SubSection>
**Read the blank position first, before you read anything else**

Knowing where the blank falls shapes how you read the paragraph. If it is at the beginning, you read the rest of the paragraph to understand what topic needs introducing. If it is in the middle, you read with attention to how the surrounding sentences connect. If it is at the end, you read for what kind of conclusion the paragraph is building towards.
</SubSection>
<SubSection>
**Eliminate options that introduce ideas the paragraph never develops**

This is the most reliable elimination rule in paragraph completion. Placement exam paragraphs are coherent units. Every sentence serves the central argument. An option that introduces a concept not present anywhere else in the paragraph is almost certainly planted as a distractor. Look for it and remove it first.
</SubSection>
<SubSection>
**Watch for tonal mismatches**

An option written in a markedly different tone from the rest of the paragraph is wrong. If the paragraph is cautious and analytical, an option that is emotionally dramatic or colloquially casual does not belong. Tone matching is a fast and reliable filter.
</SubSection>
<SubSection>
**Check pronoun references before committing to an option**

If your chosen option begins with a pronoun like 'it' or 'they', check that the noun it refers to appears clearly in the preceding sentence. If the reference is ambiguous or there is no clear referent, reconsider. Similarly, if the sentence following the blank uses a pronoun that seems to refer to something in the blank, check that your chosen option actually contains that noun.
</SubSection>
