# QUESTION TAGS
## 1. What Are Question Tags?
A question tag is a short question added to the end of a sentence.  
It is generally used to check information, confirm details, express feelings, or keep the conversation friendly and natural.

**For example:**  
“You remember her from class, don’t you?”

Question tags help conversations feel warm and interactive rather than stiff or direct.


## 2. What Question Tags Look Like
Think of a question tag as two parts working together:

**MAIN SENTENCE, + SHORT QUESTION**

Here is how it looks in a complete sentence:  
**“You are coming to the meeting today, aren’t you?”**

The tag turns the statement into a polite question.



## 3. Normal Question Tags
Normal question tags use the opposite form of the sentence:

- A positive sentence takes a negative tag.  
- A negative sentence takes a positive tag.  

These are the question tags used most often in everyday English.

### **Normal Question Tags in Complete Sentences**

| **Full Sentence** | **Why This Tag Is Used** |
|------------------|--------------------------|
| She is tired, isn’t she? | The sentence is positive, so the tag is negative. |
| They are ready, aren’t they? | The speaker wants polite confirmation. |
| He can swim, can’t he? | Modals (can, will, should…) follow the same pattern. |
| We will see her tomorrow, won’t we? | “Will” becomes “won’t” in the tag. |
| She does not eat meat, does she? | A negative sentence takes a positive tag. |
| They will not be late, will they? | The speaker is checking politely. |

## 5. Fixed Question Tags (They Never Change)
Fixed question tags do not change according to the verb or subject.  
They are extremely common in casual conversation.

Common fixed tags include: **right?**, **yeah?**, **okay?**, **no?**, **eh?**, **innit?**

### **Fixed Tag Examples in Complete Sentences**

| **Full Sentence** | **Why This Tag Is Used** |
|------------------|--------------------------|
| We are meeting at six, right? | This is a simple and friendly way to check a plan. |
| You finished the project, yeah? | This is an informal way to confirm information. |
| You will help me with this, okay? | This makes the request softer and more polite. |
| He sent the message, no? | This is used in some accents as a check. |

These are the tags students hear often in real conversation.

## 6. Why People Use Question Tags
Question tags are not only grammar; they help shape how we speak.

### **The Purposes of Question Tags with Complete Sentences**

| **Reason** | **Example** | **What the Tag Does** |
|-----------|-------------|------------------------|
| Checking information | You have met Sara before, haven’t you? | The speaker wants to confirm a detail. |
| Showing uncertainty | They should be here by now, should they not? | The speaker is unsure and is seeking reassurance. |
| Softening a command | Pass me the salt, will you? | The command becomes polite and less direct. |
| Showing emotion | So you ignored my message, did you? | The speaker expresses annoyance or disbelief. |
| Keeping conversation friendly | It is very warm today, isn’t it? | The speaker encourages a response. |

These functions make question tags an important part of natural English.


## 7. How to Form Question Tags 
You can form almost any question tag by following these simple steps:

<!-- TRUST Block (Blue) --> <div style="background-color:#e8f4ff; padding:12px;">
<br>**1. Find the helper verb in the sentence** 
   Examples: is, are, do, does, did, have, will, can, should  
<br>**2. Use the same verb in the tag** 
   Example: “She is…” → “isn’t she?”  
<br>**3. Change it to the opposite form**  
   Positive becomes negative, negative becomes positive.  
<br>**4. Match the subject with the correct pronoun**  
   Tom → he, Mary → she, the students → they, my phone → it </div>
<br>**Example in a full sentence:**  
“She is early, isn’t she?”  
“He does not like coffee, does he?”  
“They will help us, won’t they?”

## 8. Exceptions and Common Tendencies
These are special rules and natural patterns that people often forget.

| **Situation** | **Example Sentence** | **Easy Explanation** |
|--------------|----------------------|----------------------|
| “I am” uses “aren’t I?” | I am the last one, aren’t I? | “Am I not?” sounds unnatural in everyday speech. |
| Commands use “will you?” | Open the window, will you? | This makes the command sound polite. |
| “Let’s” uses “shall we?” | Let’s go now, shall we? | This is a fixed pattern in English. |
| Words like nobody, nothing, no one take a positive tag | Nobody called today, did they? | These words already have a negative meaning. |
| “used to” takes “did” in the tag | You used to live here, didn’t you? | “Used to” behaves like past simple, so tag uses did/didn’t. |
| Words like hardly, seldom, rarely take a positive tag | He hardly studies, does he? | These words make the sentence negative in meaning. |
| Words like someone, anyone, everyone, nobody take “they” | Someone left their phone, didn’t they? | The person is unknown, so “they” is commonly used. |
| Sentences starting with “there” keep “there” in the tag. | There isn’t any milk left, is there? | “There” remains the subject in the tag. |
