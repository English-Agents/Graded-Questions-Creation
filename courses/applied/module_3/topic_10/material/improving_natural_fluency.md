# What are Phrasal Verbs?

When we speak or write in English, we often use short, common expressions to describe actions in a natural way. Many of these expressions are phrasal verbs. A phrasal verb is formed with a main verb plus one or two small words called particles (such as up, out, on, off, in, with). Together, they work as one unit of meaning.

In many cases, the meaning of a phrasal verb is different from the meaning of the main verb alone. Therefore, phrasal verbs should be learnt as complete expressions and understood in context.

## Basic Structure of Phrasal Verbs

Phrasal verbs usually appear in these structures.

| Structure | Description | Examples |
|----------|-------------|----------|
| Verb + particle (adverb) | Often creates a new meaning; may be separable | turn off, pick up, give up |
| Verb + preposition | Usually inseparable; object follows the preposition | look after, rely on, deal with |
| Verb + particle + preposition | Three-part phrasal verbs; object follows the final preposition | look forward to, put up with, get away with |


## Types of Phrasal Verbs

### 1) Two-word phrasal verbs

Two-word phrasal verbs consist of a verb plus one particle.

| Phrasal Verb | Meaning | Example Sentence |
|-------------|---------|------------------|
| wake up | stop sleeping | She woke up early. |
| set off | start a journey | They set off at dawn. |
| give up | stop trying | He gave up easily. |
| turn down | reduce volume / reject | She turned down the music. |

### 2) Three-word phrasal verbs

Three-word phrasal verbs consist of a verb plus two particles.

| Phrasal Verb | Meaning | Example Sentence |
|-------------|---------|------------------|
| look forward to | feel pleased about something that will happen | I am looking forward to the holiday. |
| put up with | tolerate | She cannot put up with the noise. |
| get away with | avoid punishment | He got away with cheating. |
| come up with | produce an idea | They came up with a solution. |


## Transitive and Intransitive Phrasal Verbs

### Transitive (takes an object)

A transitive phrasal verb needs an object and can often answer the question “What?” or “Whom?”

| Phrasal Verb | Object Present? | Example Sentence |
|-------------|------------------|------------------|
| turn off | Yes | She turned off the light. |
| pick up | Yes | He picked up the parcel. |
| put off | Yes | They put off the meeting. |
| carry out | Yes | The researchers carried out a study. |


### Intransitive (no object)

An intransitive phrasal verb does not take an object.

| Phrasal Verb | Object Present? | Example Sentence |
|-------------|------------------|------------------|
| take off | No | The plane took off. |
| get up | No | I got up early. |
| sit down | No | Please sit down. |
| go out | No | The lights went out. |

## Separable and Inseparable Phrasal Verbs

### Separable phrasal verbs

Some phrasal verbs allow the object to come between the verb and the particle, or after the particle.

| Pattern | Example (noun object) | Example (pronoun object) |
|--------|------------------------|---------------------------|
| Verb + object + particle | She turned the light off. | She turned it off. |
| Verb + particle + object | She turned off the light. | Not normally used: She turned off it. |

**Common separable phrasal verbs:**

| Phrasal Verb | Meaning | Example Sentence (comparison) |
|-------------|---------|------------------------------|
| turn off | switch off | She turned the fan off. / She turned off the fan. |
| put off | postpone | They put the match off. / They put off the match. |
| pick up | collect | He picked the parcel up. / He picked up the parcel. |
| throw away | discard | She threw the papers away. / She threw away the papers. |


### Inseparable phrasal verbs

Some phrasal verbs cannot be separated. The object must follow the complete phrasal verb.

| Phrasal Verb | Meaning | Correct Example | Incorrect Pattern |
|-------------|---------|----------------|------------------|
| look after | take care of | She looked after the child. | She looked the child after. |
| rely on | depend on | You can rely on him. | You can rely him on. |
| deal with | handle | He dealt with the problem. | He dealt the problem with. |
| run into | meet unexpectedly | I ran into my teacher. | I ran my teacher into. |


## Phrasal Verbs in the Passive Voice (Important Rule)

When a phrasal verb is used in the passive voice, the verb and particle stay together.

| Active Voice | Passive Voice |
|------------|---------------|
| They called off the meeting. | The meeting was called off by them. |
| His grandmother looked after him. | He was looked after by his grandmother. |
| They sent him away to school. | He was sent away to school. |
| Someone turned off the lights. | The lights were turned off. |


## Exceptional Cases

Some verbs and verb combinations cannot function as true phrasal verbs in the ways learners often attempt, and some passive forms are avoided because they are unnatural or not grammatically possible.

### Intransitive phrasal verbs cannot be made passive

If there is no object, there is nothing to become the subject in the passive voice.

| Intransitive Phrasal Verb | Active Sentence | Incorrect Passive Form (not possible) | Why it is incorrect |
|--------------------------|----------------|----------------------------------------|--------------------|
| take off | The plane took off. | The plane was taken off. | There is no object in the active sentence. |
| get up | She got up early. | She was got up early. | There is no object to move to the subject position. |
| go out | The lights went out. | The lights were gone out. | The verb does not take an object in this meaning. |
| break down (stop working) | The car broke down. | The car was broken down. | The active structure has no object. |


### Do not separate inseparable phrasal verbs

Some learners incorrectly place the object between the verb and the preposition.

| Phrasal Verb | Correct Sentence | Incorrect Sentence | Reason |
|-------------|------------------|-------------------|--------|
| look after | She looked after the child. | She looked the child after. | The verb + preposition pattern is inseparable. |
| rely on | We rely on them. | We rely them on. | The object must follow the preposition. |
| deal with | He dealt with the matter. | He dealt the matter with. | The preposition must keep its object after it. |


### Avoid incorrect pronoun placement with separable phrasal verbs

With separable phrasal verbs, pronoun objects usually go between the verb and particle.

| Phrasal Verb | Correct with pronoun | Incorrect with pronoun | Reason |
|-------------|----------------------|-------------------------|--------|
| turn off | Turn it off. | Turn off it. | Pronoun objects normally go between verb and particle. |
| pick up | Pick them up. | Pick up them. | Pronoun placement rule for separable verbs. |
| put off | Put it off. | Put off it. | Pronoun objects rarely follow the particle. |


### Some passive forms are grammatically possible but unnatural

Some passive sentences are technically possible but avoided in good writing because they sound unnatural or unclear.

| Active Sentence | Passive Sentence (possible but unnatural) | Why it is avoided |
|---------------|-------------------------------------------|------------------|
| Someone has slept in this bed. | This bed has been slept in. | The passive is structurally possible, but it is awkward and rare in normal usage. |
| People have sat on this bench. | This bench has been sat on. | The passive exists in limited contexts but is generally avoided in careful writing. |


## Some Commonly used Phrasal Verbs

### Everyday Usage

| Phrasal Verb | Common Meaning | Example Sentence |
|-------------|----------------|------------------|
| get up | rise from bed | I get up at six o’clock on weekdays. |
| wake up | stop sleeping | She woke up suddenly during the night. |
| go out | leave home | We went out after dinner. |
| come back | return | He came back late from work. |
| look for | search for | She is looking for her keys |
| look after | take care of | He looks after his younger brother. |
| get on with | have a good relationship | I get on with my neighbours. |
| pick up | collect | I will pick up the parcel tomorrow. |
| drop off | take someone/something to a place | Can you drop me off near the station? |
| put on | wear | Put on your jacket; it is cold outside. |
| take off | remove (clothes/shoes) | Please take off your shoes. |
| run out of | have none left | We ran out of milk yesterday. |


### Academic Usage

| Phrasal Verb | Common Meaning | Example Sentence |
|-------------|----------------|------------------|
| carry out | conduct (research/experiment) | The students carried out an experiment in the lab. |
| find out | discover (facts/results) | The researcher found out the cause of the error. |
| look into | investigate | The committee will look into the matter carefully. |
| point out | indicate/mention (especially in discussion) | The author points out several limitations in the study. |
| set out | present (ideas/aims) clearly | This chapter sets out the main arguments. |
| work out | calculate/solve | The learners worked out the answers using the formula. |
| write up | prepare a written report | You must write up the findings in your notebook. |
| sum up | summarise | To sum up, the evidence supports the conclusion. |


### Professional Usage

| Phrasal Verb | Common Meaning | Example Sentence |
|-------------|----------------|------------------|
| follow up | take further action / contact again | I will follow up with the client tomorrow. |
| carry out | perform a task officially | The team carried out the safety inspection. |
| set up | arrange/establish | We need to set up a meeting with the manager. |
| sort out | resolve a problem | The accounts department sorted out the billing issue. |
| deal with | handle a situation | She dealt with the complaint politely. |
| put forward | suggest a plan/idea | He put forward a new proposal in the meeting. |
| take over | assume control/responsibility | The assistant manager will take over next month. |
| step down | leave a position | The director stepped down after five years. |
| draw up | prepare (a document) formally | They drew up the contract yesterday. |
| carry on | continue (work/operations) | We will carry on with the project as planned. |
