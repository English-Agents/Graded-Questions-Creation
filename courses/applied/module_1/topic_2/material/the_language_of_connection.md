# The Language of Connection

## Units of a Sentence
Every sentence is built from smaller components that add meaning and structure.

| **Unit**     | **Description**                                                                 | **Key Feature**                        | **Example**                                     |
|--------------|----------------------------------------------------------------------------------|----------------------------------------|-------------------------------------------------|
| **Phrase**   | A small group of words; Does not contain a complete subject–verb pair        | It cannot stand alone                  | the tired workers in the field             |
| **Clause**   | A larger unit that may include one or more phrases; Contains a subject and a verb | Only independent clauses can stand alone | Before the sun rose (dependent) / the tired workers in the field started their tasks (independent) |
| **Sentence** | The complete structure formed from clauses; Expresses a full and meaningful idea | It stands alone as a complete idea     | Before the sun rose, the tired workers in the field started their tasks. |
<br>
### Using Phrases, Clauses, and Sentences in Communication

**Phrases, clauses, and sentences** are used differently in everyday conversation. Phrases are often used in everyday speech to give quick, partial information without repeating full sentences. Clauses help us express reasons, time, or conditions. A sentence expresses a complete idea and can be understood without needing more information.

| **Unit** | **Where Do We Use It?** | **Examples** |
|:--------|:------------------------|:-------------|
| **Phrase** | To add brief information.<br><br>To respond casually or quickly.<br><br>To show tone, time, place, or feeling<br><br>Used when the context is already known. | Person A: When do we start the project?<br>Person B: After lunch.<br><br>Person A: Where did you see him?<br>Person B: At the reception desk.<br><br>Person A: How was the session?<br>Person B: Very helpful. |
| **Clause** | To express ideas, reasons, time, or conditions.<br><br>Often used in longer or complex responses.<br><br>Used when the context is already set. | Person A: Why is he frustrated?<br>Person B: Because no one replied to his email.<br><br>Person A: Will you join the workshop?<br>Person B: If my shift ends early.<br><br>Person A: Why didn’t she come today?<br>Person B: Because she wasn’t feeling well. |
| **Sentence** | To communicate complete and clear ideas.<br><br>Used in both formal and informal situations.<br><br>Can vary in tone and length based on purpose | Person A: Why are you smiling?<br>Person B: I received some great news this morning.<br><br>Person A: Are you joining the meeting?<br>Person B: I will join after I finish my report.<br><br>Person A: What happened yesterday?<br>Person B: I missed the bus, so I arrived late. |
<br>
###Types of Sentences
Different sentence structures help express ideas with clarity, accuracy, and an appropriate level of detail.
In technical communication such as reports, documentation, and workplace emails, selecting the right type of sentence improves readability and overall effectiveness.

| **Type of Sentence** | **Application (Purpose)** | **Where to Use** | **Example Sentence** |
|:---------------------|:--------------------------|:------------------|:---------------------|
| **Simple** | To give quick clarity; state one fact directly. | Alerts, error messages, step-by-step instructions, quick updates | The system is now online. |
| **Compound** | To connect two equally important points. | Team updates, daily stand-ups, progress reports, meeting notes | The system is now online, and the support team is monitoring performance. |
| **Complex** | To explain reasons, conditions, or contrasts. | Incident reports, documentation, client communication | The system restarted automatically because a security patch was installed. |
| **Compound-complex** | To provide multiple facts plus an explanation/condition. | Detailed project reports, escalation emails, technical proposals | The system restarted automatically, and users lost their unsaved work because the outage was sudden. |

<br>### Common Errors in the Use of Conjunctions and Connectors
Conjunctions and connectors help link ideas smoothly, but many learners make common mistakes when using them. The following table highlights the most frequent errors and their correct forms for easy understanding.

| **Error Type**                               | **Incorrect Sentence**                                           | **Correct Sentence(s)**                                                                                   | **Why Incorrect**                                                                                          |
|---------------------------------------------|------------------------------------------------------------------|-----------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------|------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------|
| Using “*although*” + “*but*” together           | Although it was raining, but we went outside.                    | Although it was raining, we went outside. / It was raining, but we went outside.                         | “Although” and “but” both show contrast; only one is needed.                                               |
| Using *two connectors* in one sentence        | Therefore, we had no money, so we couldn’t buy tickets.         | We had no money, so we couldn’t buy tickets. / We had no money. Therefore, we couldn’t buy tickets.      | Only one connector is needed to join ideas.                                                                |
| Using “*because*” + “*so*” together             | Because he was late, so he missed the bus.                      | Because he was late, he missed the bus. / He was late, so he missed the bus.                             | “Because” and “so” cannot be used together in the same sentence.                                           |
| Using “*despite*” with a full clause          | Despite he was tired, he finished the work.                     | Despite being tired, he finished the work. / Although he was tired, he finished the work.                | “Despite” must be followed by a noun or gerund, not a full clause.                                         |
| Using “*in spite of*” with a clause           | In spite of she studied well, she failed.                       | In spite of studying well, she failed. / Although she studied well, she failed.                          | “In spite of” must be followed by a noun or -ing form.                                                     |
| Using “*unless*” with negative verbs          | I won’t go unless you don’t come.                               | I won’t go unless you come. / I won’t go if you don’t come.                                              | “Unless” already means “if not,” so using a negative verb creates a double negative.                       |
| Confusing “*so*” and “*so that*”                | He was tired, so that he went to bed early.                     | He was tired, so he went to bed early. / He went to bed early so that he could rest.                     | “So that” expresses purpose, not result.                                                           |
| Incorrect placement of “*however*”            | She likes coffee however she doesn’t drink it daily.            | She likes coffee; however, she doesn’t drink it daily. / She likes coffee, but she doesn’t drink it daily.| “However” cannot join clauses directly; it must be separated with punctuation (comma/semicolon + however). |
| Using “*therefore*” as a conjunction          | I was hungry therefore I ate food.                              | I was hungry; therefore, I ate food. / I was hungry, so I ate food.                                      | “Therefore” is a connector (adverb), not a conjunction; it needs punctuation to link the two clauses.      |
