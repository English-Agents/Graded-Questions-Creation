# <b>Fillers and Sentence Stress</b>

When we speak English, we often use small words or short phrases to help the conversation move smoothly. These are called <b>fillers</b>. Fillers do not usually add new facts. Instead, they help the speaker start, pause, change direction, soften a reply, or check that the listener is following.

## <b>Examples:</b>

| Focus | Example | What it shows |
|---|---|---|
| Filler | <b>Well,</b> I am not sure. | Softens the reply and gives thinking time. |
| Filler | It is difficult, <b>you know?</b> | Checks shared understanding. |
| Filler | It was cheap, <b>I mean,</b> reasonable. | Clarifies and corrects meaning. |

## <b>Why are Fillers important?</b>

Fillers make speech sound natural and polite. They help speakers:

- speak smoothly without long silence
- organise ideas while speaking
- change topic or return to the main point
- respond carefully when they disagree or refuse

## <b>Where are Fillers used?</b>

| Context | How fillers are used | Example |
|---|---|---|
| Informal speech | Very common; shows friendliness and hesitation | <b>Well,</b> I do not know. |
| Formal speech | Used to organise points and transitions | <b>Now,</b> let us move to the next idea. |
| Writing | Spoken fillers are avoided; linking words are used instead | <b>Moreover,</b> the evidence is strong. |

## <b>Types of Fillers</b>

| Type | What it does | Common fillers | Example sentence |
|---|---|---|---|
| Starting or moving on | Starts a reply or changes topic | well, right, anyway | <b>Anyway,</b> let us continue. |
| Checking | Checks if the listener is following | you know?, you see? | It is clear, <b>you see?</b> |
| Correcting or clarifying | Fixes or explains meaning | I mean, actually | <b>Actually,</b> I meant Tuesday. |

## <b>Placement of Fillers in Speech</b>

Fillers often appear in predictable places.

| Position | Example | Why it is used |
|---|---|---|
| Beginning | <b>Well,</b> I am not certain. | Soft start; thinking time |
| Middle | It is, <b>I mean,</b> quite difficult. | Clarification |
| End | It is cold, <b>isn’t it?</b> | Checks agreement |

# <b>Sentence Stress</b>

## <b>What is Sentence Stress?</b>

In spoken English, we do not pronounce every word with the same strength. We naturally stress the words that carry the main meaning. This is called <b>sentence stress</b>. Sentence stress helps listeners understand what is important.

## <b>Examples:</b>

| Focus | Example | What it shows |
|---|---|---|
| Sentence stress | I <b>don’t</b> like it. | Stress on ‘<b>don’t</b>’ shows contrast. |
| Sentence stress | I asked <b>you</b>, not him. | Stress corrects an assumption. |

## <b>Why is Sentence Stress important?</b>

Sentence stress makes speech easier to follow because it highlights the key message. It helps listeners notice:

- the most important information
- what is new in the message
- what is being corrected or contrasted

## <b>Core idea of Sentence Stress</b>

In most sentences, we stress the words that carry the main meaning. These are often <b>nouns, main verbs, adjectives, and adverbs</b>. Small grammar words (such as articles, pronouns, and prepositions) are often weaker.

| Word type | Usually stressed? | Examples |
|---|---|---|
| Meaning words | Yes | report, finished, important, quickly |
| Grammar words | Usually no | a, the, to, for, can, was, he, they |

## <b>Examples:</b>

| Sentence (stressed words highlighted) | Main message |
|---|---|
| She <b>finished</b> the <b>report yesterday</b>. | completion + time |
| They <b>changed</b> the plan <b>quickly</b>. | action + speed |

## <b>Two common stress patterns</b>

| Pattern | Meaning | Example |
|---|---|---|
| Main stress | The strongest stress falls on the key new word | I need a new <b>laptop</b>. |
| Contrast stress | Stress is used to correct or contrast | I asked <b>you</b>. |

## <b>How stress changes focus</b>

Stress does not change the grammar, but it changes what feels most important.

| Sentence | Likely main stress | Focus |
|---|---|---|
| I bought a new <b>phone</b>. | phone | new item |
| I bought a <b>new</b> phone. | new | contrast (not old) |

## <b>Simple note on fast speech</b>

In natural speech, small grammar words are often said more quickly and less clearly. This helps the important words stand out.

| Word | When it is said clearly | When it is said quickly | Example (clear) | Example (normal) |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| can | when emphasised | in a normal statement | <b>Can</b> you swim? | I can swim. |
| to | when emphasised | in a normal statement | I do not want <b>to</b>. | We went to school. |
| of | when emphasised | in a normal phrase | What is it made <b>of?</b> | a cup of tea |

## <b>Exception Cases and Restrictions</b>

Small grammar words are usually weaker, but they become strong in certain cases.

| Case | Example | Why it happens |
|---|---|---|
| Negative forms | I <b>can’t</b> agree. | Negatives are important and stand out |
| Emphasis for truth | I <b>did</b> send it. | The speaker insists it is true |
| End-position words | I do not know who it is <b>for</b>. | The final word can receive stress |

## <b>Example</b>

### <b>General usage</b>

| Focus | Example | What it shows |
|---|---|---|
| Fillers | <b>Well,</b> I am not sure. | Softens the reply. |
| Fillers | <b>Actually,</b> I cannot come today. | Polite correction. |
| Sentence stress | I <b>did</b> finish it. | Confirms the truth. |
| Sentence stress | I asked <b>you</b>, not your brother. | Corrects an assumption. |

### <b>Academic usage</b>

| Focus | Example | What it shows |
|---|---|---|
| Fillers | <b>Now,</b> let us consider the next point. | Organises the explanation. |
| Fillers | <b>Actually,</b> the evidence suggests the opposite. | Careful correction. |
| Sentence stress | The <b>main finding</b> is important. | Highlights the key idea. |
| Sentence stress | This method is <b>more reliable</b>. | Supports comparison. |

### <b>Professional usage</b>

| Focus | Example | What it shows |
|---|---|---|
| Fillers | <b>Right,</b> shall we move on? | Signals a transition. |
| Fillers | <b>Well,</b> the safest option is to postpone it. | Softens a recommendation. |
| Sentence stress | We need the report by <b>Friday</b>. | Focuses on the deadline. |
| Sentence stress | I <b>did</b> send the email yesterday. | Confirms action clearly. |
