#Tenses Ability

The phrase **""able to""** is used to express ability, and it changes slightly depending on whether you are talking about the present, past, or future. It is used to show someone's ability to do something.

##Present Tense

When you use **""able to**"" in the present tense, it means that **someone has the ability to do something right now**. You can use it with the verb **""am/is/are""** depending on the person whom we are talking about.

###Structure

###1. Positive Sentences

Using **""am""**,

I + **am + able to + verb (I form)** + rest of the sentence.

**Examples**

- I **am able to solve** this math problem.
- I **am able to fix** the computer right now.
- I **am able to cook** dinner for a small group of people.

Using **”is”**,

She/ He/ This/ That/ any singular noun (except you) + **is + able to + verb (I form)** + rest of the sentence.

**Examples**

- He **is able to lift** heavy weights.
- She **is able to speak** three languages fluently.
- The cat **is able to climb** the tree quickly.

Using **”are”**

You/ We/ They/ These/ Those/ any plural noun + **are + able to + verb (I form)** + rest of the sentence.

**Examples**

- They **are able to work** together effectively.
- We **are able to finish** the project today.
- You **are able to play** the piano beautifully.

###2. Negative Sentences

Using **”am not”**,

I + **am not + able to + verb (I form)** + rest of the sentence.

**Examples**

- I **am not able to solve** this math problem.
- I **am not able to fix** the computer right now.
- I **am not able to cook** dinner for a small group of people.

Using **”is not”**,

She/ He/ This/ That/ any singular noun (except you) + **is not + able to + verb (I form)** + rest of the sentence.

**Examples**

- He **is not able to lift** heavy weights.
- She **is not able to speak** three languages fluently.
- The cat **is not able to climb** the tree quickly.

Using **”are not”**

You/ We/ They/ These/ Those/ any plural noun + **are not + able to + verb (I form)** + rest of the sentence.

**Examples**

- They **are not able to work** together effectively.
- We **are not able to finish** the project today.
- You **are not able to play** the piano beautifully.


##Past Continuous

###Definition

The past continuous tense describes actions that were **happening at a specific time in the past**. These actions were ongoing in the past but may have been interrupted or stopped later.

###Structure

####1. Positive Sentences

Using **”was”**

I/ He / She / It / Singular nouns (except you)/ This/ That + **was + verb (-ing form)** + rest of the sentence.

**Examples**

- I **was reading** a book when the lights went out.
- She **was cooking** dinner at 7 PM.
- Rahul **was cleaning** the house all morning.

Using **”were”**

You/ We / Plural nouns / They/ These/ Those + **were + verb (-ing form)** + rest of the sentence.

**Examples**

- You **were working** late last night.
- They **were playing** soccer when it started to rain.
- The students **were discussing** their project after class.

####2. Negative Sentences

Using **”was not”**

I/ He / She / It / Singular nouns (except you)/ This/ That + **was not** + verb (-ing form) + rest of the sentence.

**Examples**

- I **was not jogging** in the park yesterday morning.
- She **was not watering** the plants in the garden yesterday.
- The dog **was not barking** at strangers last night.

Using **”were not”**

You/ We / Plural nouns / They/ These/ Those + **were not + verb (-ing form)** + rest of the sentence.

**Examples**

- You **were not studying** for the test at 9 PM.
- They **were not rehearsing** for the play last evening.
- The children **were not swimming** in the pool when the rain started.
