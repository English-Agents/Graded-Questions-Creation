# Future Simple 1


##Future Simple: Informing about the Future

In the future tense, **will** is commonly used to talk about actions, events, or situations that are expected to happen in the future. It is one of the most straightforward ways to express the future.

###Sentence Structure

###1. Positive Sentences
The sentence structure for using future tense to talk about actions, events, or situations that are expected to happen in the future,

```
I/ you/ we/ any plural noun/singular noun/ they/that/ this/those/these/ he/ she/ it + **will + verb (I form)** + rest of the sentence.
```
**Examples**

- He **will start** a new job next month.
- I **will help** you with your assignment later.
- They **will visit** their grandparents during the summer.
- The cat **will stay** at home while we are away.
- We **will organize** a party for her birthday next week.

###2. Negative Sentences

The sentence structure for using future tense to talk about actions, events, or situations **that are not expected to happen in the future**,

```
I/ you/ we/ any plural noun/singular noun/ they/that/ this/those/these/ he/ she/ it + **will not + verb (I form)** + rest of the sentence.
```
**Examples**

- I **will not eat** dessert tonight.
- They **will not participate** in the competition this year.
- The movie **will not start** until 8 PM.
- The weather **will not improve** by tomorrow.
- We **will not finish** the assignment on time.

```
Note: The form of the verb does not change depending on who or what does the action. 
Even if it is plural or singular, the verb (I form) will be used in the case of simple future tense sentences using “will”.
```
**Everyday Conversation Examples**

- A: Do you think it will rain tomorrow?<br>B: I don’t think so. The forecast says it **will not rain**, and it **will stay** sunny all day.
- A: Will they join us for dinner?<br>B: They **will join** if they finish work early, but if not, they **will not make** it.
- A: Do you think he will call us back?<br>B: He **will call** if he gets the message, but he **will not reply** if he is still in the meeting.


#Present Perfect Tense: Sentence Structure

Sentences in present perfect tense follow two structures.

1. Using “has” and “have”

2. Using “has been” and “have been”

##Using “Has” and “Have”

###Using “Has”

**1. Positive Sentences**

```
He/ She/ It/ any singular noun/this/ that + has + verb III form
```

**Examples**

- She *has baked* a delicious cake.

- Rahul *has started* a new job recently.

- Maria *has written* three books.

**2. Negative Sentences**


```

He/ She/ It/ any singular noun/this/ that + has not + verb III form

```

**Examples**

- John *has not cleaned* his room yet.

- Lisa *has not arrived* at the party.

- The dog *has not eaten* its food.


###Using “Have”

**1. Positive Sentences**

```
I/you/ we/ any plural noun/ those/ these + have + verb III form

```
**Examples**

- We *have completed* the project on time.

- They *have visited* the zoo several times.

- I *have bought* a new laptop recently.

**2. Negative Sentences**

```
I/you/ we/ any plural noun/ those/ these + have not + verb III form

```

**Examples**

- We *have not received* any updates yet.

- They *have not watched* the movie.

- I *have not finished* my breakfast.

---

**Present Perfect Tense Conversation Examples**

A: Have you finished your assignment?

B: Yes, *I have submitted* it already.

A: Has she told you about the meeting?

B: No, *she hasn’t mentioned* anything yet.

A: *They have completed* the project on time.

B: That’s great! The client must be happy.
