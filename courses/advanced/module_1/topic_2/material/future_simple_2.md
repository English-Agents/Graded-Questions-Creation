# Future Simple 2

#Future Simple: Confirmed Immediate Future Plans

##Definition

Future simple sentences are used to talk about the **immediate future**, specifically when discussing plans, intentions, or things that seem **likely or confirmed to happen**. 

**“Going to”** is the keyword here.

##Structure

###1. Positive Sentences

The sentences in future tense to talk about the immediate future follow the structure,

**Using “am”**

```
I + am + going to + verb I form

```

**Examples**

- I **am going to start** a new book tonight.

- I **am going to buy** groceries after work.

- I **am going to call** my friend later today.

- I **am going to clean** my room tomorrow.

- I **am going to apply** for a new job next month.

**Using “is”**

```

He/ She/ It/ any singular noun (except you) / this / that + is + going to + verb I form

```

**Examples**

- The dog **is going to get** a bath this evening.

- It **is going to rain** later today.

- She **is going to move** to a new apartment next week.

- The teacher **is going to explain** the new topic in the next class.
 
- The baby **is going to sleep** soon.

**Using “are”**

```
You/ We/ any plural noun/ Those/ These + are + going to + verb I form

```

**Examples**

- The students **are going to present** their projects next week.

- The athletes **are going to compete** in the championship tomorrow.

- You **are going to enjoy** the concert tonight.

- We **are going to clean up** the park this weekend.

- They **are going to celebrate** their anniversary next month.

**Conversation Examples**

- A: Is the company going to launch the new product this quarter, or are there still delays?<br>B: They **are going to launch** it next month, but they **are going to delay** some features until the following update.

- A: Are you and your team going to finish the project on time despite the setbacks?<br>B: We **are going to work** overtime if necessary, and we **are going to prioritize** the most critical tasks.

- A: Are you going to submit the report by Friday, or will you need an extension?<br>B: I **am going to try** to finish it by then, but I **am also going to ask** for a backup plan just in case.

- A: Is she going to present the findings herself, or will someone else take over?<br>B: She **is going to lead** the presentation, and she **is going to answer** all the questions afterward.

###2. Negative Sentences

Some sentences in future tense can also be used to express **negative future actions or intentions**. 

They indicate that someone does not plan to do something, does not intend for an action to happen, or that an event is not expected to occur. 

**Using “am not”**

```
I + am not + going to + verb I form

```

**Examples**

- I **am not going to buy** that new phone; it’s too expensive.
 
- I **am not going to change** my plans just because of the rain.
 
- I **am not going to take** the job offer.
 
- I **am not going to tell** anyone about the surprise party.
 
- I **am not going to travel** this weekend because I have too much work.


**Using “is not”**

```
He/ She/ It/ any singular noun (except you) / this / that + is not + going to + verb I form

```


**Examples**

- The chef **is not going to alter** the recipe for the dish.
 
- The artist **is not going to exhibit** his new paintings this year.
 
- The company **is not going to relocate** its headquarters.
 
- She **is not going to forgive** him easily after what happened.
 
- He **is not going to attend** the seminar this weekend.


**Using “are not”**

```
You/ We/ any plural noun/ Those/ These + are not + going to + verb I form

```

**Examples**

- They **are not going to tolerate** such behavior any longer.

- The players **are not going to underestimate** their opponents this time.

- We **are not going to abandon** the project halfway through.

- You **are not going to disrupt** the meeting with any more interruptions.
 
- The teachers **are not going to compromise** on the quality of education.

**Conversation Examples**

- A: The kids **are going to join** the cleanup effort tomorrow.<br>B: That’s great, but they **are not going to stay** for the whole time because they have another event.

- A: The committee **is going to review** the proposal on Friday.<br>B: That’s good, but they **are not going to approve** until all questions are answered..

- A: I **am going to make** dinner tonight.<br>B: Sounds delicious, but I **am not going to eat** too much because I’m on a diet.

---

>**Note:** 

>For future simple sentences about the immediate future, **“going to” will always be followed by a verb (I form)**. 

>If “going to” is followed by words that are not verbs, then the sentence will not be in future tense. 

>For example, in the sentence “She is going to school”, “going to” is not followed by a verb, but by a noun “school” and thus, this sentence is not in future tense.
