# Conditional Sentences and Hypotheticals

# Conditional sentences
Conditional sentences are sentences that express a situation where one event depends on another. They are made up of two parts: The part of the sentence that presents the condition (often starting with 'if') and the result of that condition.
`Condition (if) part + result part`

## Types of Conditional Sentences
### 1. Conditional sentences can be used to express imagined conditions.
| Level of Certainty     | Conditional Type   | Structure                                                                                                                | Example                                                                                   | Explanation                                                                                  |
|------------------------|--------------------|--------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------|-------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------|----------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------|
| Possible or Likely     | First Conditional  | If + present simple, modal verb used to express future meaning (shall/should/will/would/can/could/may/might) + base verb | If the company offers a better package, I will join it.                                   | It is possible or likely that the company will offer a better package.                       |
| Unlikely               | Second Conditional | If + past simple, (should/would/might/could) + base verb                                                                 | If the company offered a better package, I would join it.                                 | It is not likely that the company will offer a better package.                               |
| Impossible             | Third Conditional  | If + past perfect, modal (should/would/might/could) + have + past participle verb                                        | If the company had offered a better package, I would have joined it.                      | The company did not offer a better package, so joining the company is now impossible.        |

### 1.1 First Conditional for Real Future Possibilities

We use first conditional to express the result of an imagined future situation, when we believe the imagined situation is a possible event in the future if a certain condition is met.  

| First Conditional Sentence | Imagined Condition | Result | Explanation |
|----------|--------------------|--------|-------------|
| If it rains, we will stay indoors. | If it rains | We will stay indoors | The rain is a possible condition, and if it happens, staying indoors is the likely result. |

## Examples of First Conditional Sentences  
| Sentence | Condition | Result |
|----------|-----------|--------|
| If you are focused, you will achieve your goal. | If you are focused | You will achieve your goal |
| If you are not focused, you will not achieve your goal. | If you are not focused | You will not achieve your goal |
| If April 30th is a Friday, then May 1st will be a Saturday. | If April 30th is a Friday | May 1st will be a Saturday |


<MultiLineNote>
<strong></strong>We use the modal verb in the <em>result part</em> of the sentence, not in the 'if' part.  

<strong>Example:</strong>  
✅ If the company offers a better package, I will join it.  
❌ If the company will offer a better package, I will join it.
</MultiLineNote>



<MultiLineNote style="background-color:#d4edda; padding:10px; border-radius:6px; border:1px solid #c3e6cb;">
Conditional sentences can start with either the 'if' part or the result.  

| Sentence | Punctuation Rule |
|----------|-------------|
| If I die, will you marry someone else? | Conditional part first; a comma is normally used in writing |
| I will pay you double if you get the work finished by Friday. | Conditional part second; a comma is not normally used in writing |

This rule applies to every type of conditional sentence.
</MultiLineNote>


<MultiLineNote style="background-color:#d4edda; padding:10px; border-radius:6px; border:1px solid #c3e6cb;">
<strong></strong>Conditional sentences can start with either the 'if' part or the result.  

<strong>Example 1:</strong> If I die, will you marry someone else? <em>(Conditional part first; a comma is normally used in writing)</em>  

<strong>Example 2:</strong> I will pay you double if you get the work finished by Friday. <em>(Conditional part second; a comma is not normally used in writing)</em>  

This rule applies to every type of conditional sentence.
</MultiLineNote>


### 1.2 Second Conditional for Hypothetical Situations

The second conditional is used to express hypothetical or unlikely situations in the present or future.  
It imagines what would happen if a certain condition were true, even if that condition is unlikely to happen.

| Example | Explanation |
|----------|-------------|
| If we lived closer to the beach, we would go swimming every day. | The condition — living closer to the beach — is imagined. If it were true, the result — swimming every day — would happen. |
| If I were taller, I would be playing basketball professionally. | The condition — being taller — is imagined. If it were true, the result — playing basketball professionally — would happen. |
<MultiLineNote style=""background-color:#cce5ff; padding:10px; border-radius:6px; border:1px solid #99ccff;"">
<strong></strong>  

- The 'if' part uses the past simple tense, and the result part uses would + base form of the verb. 
  <strong>The condition and the result are not actually in the past — the past tense shows the unreal or imagined nature of the situation.</strong>   

- For imagined or hypothetical situations, we use <strong>'were'</strong> instead of 'was'.

| Expression | Usage | Examples |
|------------|-------|----------|
| **If I were** | Used for hypothetical or unreal situations (wishes, dreams, advice). | If I were you, I would take that job.<br>If she were here, she would help us.|
| **If I was** | Used for past situations that were possible, uncertain, or might have happened. | I'm not sure if I was right or wrong to end the relationship.<br>I'm sorry if I was rude. |


- We use the modal verb in the <strong>result part</strong> of the sentence, not in the 'if' part.  
  <br>✅ If she spoke Spanish, she would get a job in Spain.  
  <br>❌ If she would speak Spanish, she would get a job in Spain.  
</MultiLineNote>



### 1.3 Third Conditional for Past Situations That Didn't Happen (Past Unreal Conditional)

The third conditional is used to express a situation that **did not happen in the past** and imagine what the result would have been **if the condition had been met**.

| Example | Explanation |
|----------|-------------|
| If I had studied harder, I would have passed the exam. | This means that the speaker did not study hard enough in the past and, as a result, they did not pass. If they had studied harder, the outcome would have been different. |

<div style="background-color:#cce5ff; padding:10px; border-radius:6px; border:1px solid #99ccff;">
<strong>Note:</strong><br>
We use would (modal) + have + past participle in the result part of the sentence, not in the 'if' part.<br>
✅ If I had studied harder, I would have passed the exam.<br>
❌ <em>(incorrect)</em> If I would have studied harder, I would have passed the exam.
</div>


---

### Usage of Modals in Third Conditional

| Structure | Usage | Example |
|-----------|-------|---------|
| Would + have + past participle | Used to express a certain outcome in the past. | If I had known it was your birthday, I would not have missed your party. |
| Should + have + past participle | Used to express something that was expected or supposed to happen in the past. | If I had known about the party, I should have brought a gift. |
| Should + not + have + past participle | Expresses something that was not expected or supposed to happen in the past. | If he had listened to the instructions, he should not have been confused. |
| Could + have + past participle | Implies ability or possibility in the past — suggests the action could have been done or was possible. | If I had known about the job opening, I could have applied. |
| Could + not + have + past participle | Implies inability or impossibility in the past. | If you had not helped me, I could not have avoided getting lost. |
| Might + have + past participle | Used to express something that could have happened but is not certain. | If we had left earlier, we might have caught the train. |



## 2. Conditional Sentences

### 2.1 Zero Conditionals or Real Conditionals
The zero conditionals or real conditionals refer to things that are true, that have happened, or are very likely to happen as a rule or general habit.  
It is used to express that one thing always causes another because the result is certain.

| Zero Conditional Sentence | Condition | Result | Explanation |
|----------|-----------|--------|-------------|
| If you do not add enough wood, the fire goes out. | If you do not add enough wood | The fire goes out | It is always true that the fire goes out if, or every time, you do not add enough wood. |

**Note:** Here **'if'** means **'every time.'**


> ## Verb Forms in Real Conditionals
>
> Use **Present Simple** or **Present Continuous** → for present/future real situations.  
> Use **Past Simple** or **Past Continuous** → for real past situations or past habits.  
>
> ---
>
> **Examples**  
> If I do not get enough sleep, I get a headache.  
> If a polygon has four sides, then it is a quadrilateral.  
> If the company is increasing its profits by 10%, it is expanding rapidly.  
> If the factory is producing more units, then the demand is increasing.  
> If I had free time, I always visited my parents. *(past habit)*  
> Kevin always came in to say hello if he was going past our house. *(past habit)*



---

## 3. Mixed Conditionals
We use mixed conditionals to express actions that happened or did not happen in the past, but have results which continue or are still important in the present.

**Structure:**  
`If + past perfect, modal + base verb`

| Sentence | Explanation |
|----------|-------------|
| She wouldn’t still be working for us if we hadn’t given her a pay-rise. | *(We gave her a pay-rise → she is still working now)* *(Past event → present result)* |
| If I had studied harder in school, I would have a better job now. | *(I didn’t study harder → I don’t have a better job now)* *(Past event → present result)* |

##Conditionals: Other Expressions
| Expression | Usage | Example |
|------------|-------|---------|
| **As long as** | Used to introduce a possible or unreal situation or condition. (Same meaning as 'if') | As long as you are focussed, you will achieve your goal.  |
| **Provided that** | Used to introduce a possible or unreal situation or condition. (Same meaning as 'if')| Provided that you are focussed, you will achieve your goal. |
| **Supposing** | Can be used in place of 'if' (especially in formal language) to introduce a condition. | Supposing we go to the beach tomorrow, do you think it will rain? |
|  |  | Supposing you win the lottery, what would you do? |
|  |  | I would love to see you on Saturday, supposing I do not have to work that day. |
| **Unless** | Means something similar to *'if not'* or *'except if'*. | The employee needs to serve 60 days’ notice period unless their contract states otherwise. |
|  |  | Unless you are focussed, you will not achieve your goal. (If you are not focussed, you will not achieve your goal) |
| **Had (inversion)** | We can use **had + person/ thing + verb** instead of *if* in **third conditional** sentences. | Had I known you were waiting outside, I would have invited you in. (= If I had known …) | 
|  |  | Had she prepared better, she would have aced the exam. (= If she had prepared …) | 
| **If + were to / Were + inversion** | It can be used to talk about unlikely or hypothetical situations. | If the Prime Minister were to resign, there would have to be a general election within 30 days. |
|  |  | Were we to give up the fight now, it would mean the end of democracy in our country. (= If we gave up …) |
|  |  | Were the economy to slow down too quickly, there would be major problems. (= If the economy slowed down …) |

### Using 'When' Instead of 'If'

To describe situations that happen regularly or are expected to occur, we can use either 'if' or 'when' followed by the present tense of the verb.  

**Examples:**
Babies cry when they are hungry and sick.  
When I eat spicy food, I get a headache.

<div style=""""""""background-color:#f8d7da; padding:10px; border-radius:6px; border:1px solid #f5c6cb; color:#721c24;"""""""">
<strong>Note:</strong><br>
We do <strong>not</strong> use 'when' to introduce possible or unreal situations.<br><br>
✅ Unfortunately, if you forget your ID card, you are not permitted to enter the library.<br>
❌ Unfortunately, when you forget your ID card, you are not permitted to enter the library.
</div>


---

### Using 'Even If'
Even if is used to introduce a condition that does not change the result, no matter how the condition turns out.

**Examples:**
Even if she apologizes, I won’t forgive her.  
Even if I study all night, I still might not pass the exam.  
Even if she had worked harder, she could not have avoided the failure.

---

### Using 'If' with 'Will' and 'Would'
'Will' and 'would' can be used in conditional parts to show willingness or to talk about possible results.

<MultiLineNote>

| Purpose               | Example Sentence                                                       | Meaning / Explanation                                                   |
|-----------------------|------------------------------------------------------------------------|--------------------------------------------------------------------------|
| **Willingness**       | If you will attend the meeting, it will be more productive.            | You are willing to attend the meeting.                                  |
| **Referring to Results** | If it will make you happy, I’ll stay at home tonight.                  | If staying at home makes you happy, I will do it.                       |
| **Doubt**             | If it really would solve the problem, I would try the new method.      | I doubt the new method will work, but if it does, I would try it.       |

</MultiLineNote>

---

### Using 'If' with 'Should'
We can use 'if' with 'should' to talk about events that might happen by chance or accident.

**Example:**
If you should hear anything about the meeting, please inform me.  
*(If by chance you hear anything about the meeting.)*
