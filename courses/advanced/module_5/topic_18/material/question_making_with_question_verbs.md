# Question Making with Question Verbs

#Question Making with Question Verbs

We use modal verbs, auxiliary verbs and be verbs as question words to make questions.

**Examples**

- **Is** she coming to the meeting today?
- **Can** you help me with this task?
- **Did** they submit the report on time?
- **Have** you completed your assignment?
- **Are** they ready to present their findings?

Making questions using question verbs can be divided into three sections:

1. Questions about others
2. Questions when you are the speaker
3. Questions when you are the listener

##1. Questions about Others

##Positive Questions

To make questions using question verbs about something or someone else, follow these steps:

1. Identify the question verb in the sentence.

    For example, look at the sentence given below.

    She is eating apples.

    Here, “is” (be-verb) is the question verb.

2. Bring the **question verb** to the beginning of the sentence and capitalise its first letter. Put a question mark at the end.

    In the sentence “she is eating apples”, the question verb “is” moves to the beginning of the sentence.

    She **is** eating apples. → **Is** she eating apples?
    
(**Note:** Answers to such questions usually begin with **yes** or **no** depending on the context.)

**Examples**

- **Was** he satisfied with the project results? → Yes, he **was** satisfied with the project results.
- **Should** we discuss this issue now? → Yes, we **should** discuss this issue now.
- **Did** she know about the upcoming event? → Yes, she **did know** about the upcoming event.

##Negative Questions

In the case of negative questions,

- The word “not” is placed **after** the question verb and the subject of the question, if it is not a contraction.

    For example, look at the sentence below.

    **She is not eating** apples. → **Is she not** eating apples?

- In case of contraction, the word “not” is placed **between** the question verb and the subject.

    **She is not eating** apples.  → **Isn’t she eating** apples?

**Examples**

- **Is she not** attending the meeting today?
 No, she is not attending the meeting today.
Yes, she is attending the meeting today.

- **Was he not** supposed to lead the presentation?
No, he was not supposed to lead the presentation.
Yes, he was supposed to lead the presentation.

- **Will she not** need help with the assignment?
No, she will not need help with the assignment.
Yes, she will need help with the assignment.

- **Wouldn't it** be better to reschedule the appointment?
No, it wouldn’t be better to reschedule the appointment.
Yes, it would be better to reschedule the appointment.

- **Hasn’t she** responded to the email?
No, she hasn’t responded to the email.
Yes, she has responded to the email.


##2. Questions when you are the Speaker

Look at the following conversations:

- You (Speaker): **Are you** happy?
Person A (Listener): Yes, **I am** happy.

- You (Speaker): Is this **your** bag?
Person A (Listener): Yes, this is **my** bag.

- You (Speaker): **Were you** available for yesterday’s meeting?
Person A (Listener): Yes, **I was** available.

- You (Speaker): Is this car **yours**?
Person A (Listener): Yes, this car is **mine**.

###How to Make Questions?

1. Identify the **question verb** in the beginning.

    Person A (Listener): Yes, **I am coming** for the party.

    Here, the question verb is “am”.

2. Take all the **pronouns and question verbs** related to the listener and **change their form**.

    Here, the pronoun used by Person A (listener) is **“I”**, which changes to **”you”**.
    The question verb **“am”** changes to **are”**.

    I → You
    Am → Are

3. Place the question verb, which is changed, **before** the changed subject noun and change the capitalisation. **Put a question mark** at the end.

    **I am** coming to the party → **Are you** coming to the party?

    You (speaker): Are you coming to the party?
    Person A (listener): Yes, I am coming to the party.

**To Form Answers**

You have to reverse these steps to form answers from such questions.  Follow these steps:

1. Identify the question verb in the beginning.
2. Take the question verb after the subject noun and change the capitalisation.
3. Make all the nouns/pronouns which are related to the speaker opposite.
4. Add yes or no and replace the question mark with full stop at the end of the sentence.


**Examples**

- You (speaker): **Were you** busy?
Person A (listener): No, **I was** not busy.

- You (speaker): Was that book **yours**?
Person A (listener): Yes, that book was mine.

- You (speaker): Is she **your** best friend?
Person A (listener): No, she is not **my** best friend.

##3. Questions when you are the Listener

Look at the following conversation:

- Person A (Speaker): **Am I** disturbing **you**? 
<br>You (Listener): No, **you are** not disturbing **me**.


Certain pronouns and helping verbs change their form when questions are **asked to you**. You have to use these words accordingly in the response or while making questions.

Refer to the previous table to understand how these words change their form.

###How to Make Questions?

1. Identify the **question verb** in the beginning.
2. Take all the **pronouns and question verbs** related to the listener and **change their form**.
3. Place the question verb, which is changed, before the changed subject noun and change the capitalisation. Put a question mark at the end.

**To Form Answers**

1. Identify the question verb in the beginning.
2. Take the question verb after the subject noun and change the capitalisation.
3. Make all the nouns/pronouns which are related to the speaker opposite.
4. Add yes or no and replace the question mark with full stop at the end of the sentence.

**Examples**

- Person A (speaker): **Are you** attending the seminar on renewable energy tomorrow?
You (listener): No, **I am not** attending the seminar.

- Person A (speaker): Were **your** friends able to reach the airport on time?
You (listener): Yes, **my** friends were able to reach the airport on time.
