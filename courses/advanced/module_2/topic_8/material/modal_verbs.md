# Modal Verbs

#Modal Verbs
Modal verbs are special types of helping verbs that work with main verbs to express different meanings, such as **ability, possibility, permission, requests, advice, or necessity**. 

Modal verbs are always **used with the base form of a verb** (like go, see, or do), and they **don’t change their form** based on who/ what is doing the action.

The most common modal verbs are can, could, may, might, will, would, shall, should and must.

##May and Might

"May" and "Might" are modal verbs used to talk about possibility or to ask for permission. 

##May
###1. Positive Sentences
The sentence structure followed is,

I/ You/ She/ He/ It/ That/ They/ We/ Those/ any singular or plural noun + **may + verb (I form)**

**Talking about Possibility**

We use **"may"** when something is **highly likely to happen**, but we’re not completely sure.

```
Examples
 - It may rain later. (There’s a good chance it will rain, but it’s not certain.)
 - She may come to the party. (It’s possible she will attend, but it’s not definite.)
 ```
 
 **Asking for or Giving Permission**
 
 **"May"** is also used in a **polite way** to ask for or give permission.
 
 ```
Examples
 - May I use your phone?
 
 - You may leave the room.
 ```
 
 ###2. Negative Sentences
 The sentence structure followed is,
 I/ You/ She/ He/ It/ That/ They/ We/ Those/ any singular or plural noun + **may not + verb (I form)**
 
 **Expressing Uncertainty or Doubt**
 
 **"May not"** is used to show the possibility or uncertainty that something will not occur.
 
 ```
 Examples
 - She may not come to the party tonight.
 
 - The event may not happen if it keeps raining.
 
 - The store may not have the item in stock when we arrive.
 
 ```
 
 **Denying Permission**
 
 **"May not”** is used to **politely** deny permission.
 
 ```
 Examples
 - You may not enter the room until the meeting is over.
 - Students may not leave the classroom without permission from the teacher.
 - Visitors may not take photographs inside the museum.
 ```
 
 ###3. Questions

 **"May"** is used in questions as a polite way of requesting permission to do something.
 
 ```
 Examples
 - May I leave the room? (A polite way of asking if it is okay to leave.)
 - May we begin the meeting now? (A formal way to ask if it’s appropriate to start.)
 
 ```


 ##Might
“Might" is used to express uncertainty or doubt about whether something will happen. It suggests that **something is less likely or that there is a less possibility** it will occur, but the outcome is not certain. It’s similar to "may," but it often feels less definite and more tentative.

###1. Positive Sentences

The sentence structure followed is,

I/ You/ She/ He/ It/ That/ They/ We/ Those/ any singular or plural noun + **might + verb (I form)**

 ```
 Examples
 - It might rain later. 
 - He might come to the meeting.
 ```
 ###2. Negative Sentences
 The sentence structure followed is,
 
 I/ You/ She/ He/ It/ That/ They/ We/ Those/ any singular or plural noun + **might not + verb (I form)**
 
 ```
 Examples
 - She might not attend the conference next week.
 - The train might not arrive on time if the weather gets worse.
 - I might not finish the project before the deadline.
 ```
 **Difference Between "May" and "Might"**
 
 - **"May"**: Suggests a **higher chance of something happening** and is also used for polite permission.
 
 - **"Might"**: Suggests a **lower chance of something happening**, making it sound more uncertain.


 #Can and Could
##Can

"Can" is a modal verb commonly used to **express ability**. It indicates that someone has the skill, knowledge, or power to do something. 

"Can” is also used to **ask permissions or give permissions**. It is used to make simple requests, often in a polite manner.

###Structure

**1. Positive Sentences**

I/ You/ She/ He/ It/ They/ We/ any noun + **can + verb (I form)**

```
Examples

 Can I borrow your book for a day? (asking permission)
 
 Can you help me with this project? (request)
 
 You can use my computer for your assignment. (giving permission)
 
 Dolphins can swim at high speeds. (ability)
 
 ```
 
 **Note:** For asking permissions, we often start the question with "can" followed by who is asking (like "I") and what action they want to do. For example, “Can I open the window?”
 
 **2. Negative Sentences**
 
 We use "cannot" (or its contraction "can't") when we want to express **the inability or impossibility to do something**.
 
 The sentence structure followed is,
 
 I/ You/ She/ He/ It/ They/ We/ any noun + **cannot + verb (I form)**
 
 
 ```
 Examples
 - I cannot swim very well.
 - You cannot use your phone during the test.
 - Birds cannot breathe underwater.
 ```
 
 ##Could

 "Could" is a modal verb that is used in two main ways: to talk about past abilities and to make polite requests. 
 
 ###Past Abilities

 "Could" expresses **an ability that someone had in the past**. It is the past form of "can" when referring to someone’s capability or skill at a previous time.
 
 **1. Positive Sentences**
 
 The sentence structure followed is,
 
 I/ You/ He/ She/ It/ They/ We/ any noun + **could + verb (I form)**
 
 ```
 Examples
 
 When I was a child, I could swim very fast.
 
 He could run very fast before his injury.
 
 She could ride a bike without training wheels when she was only five.
 
 ```
 
 **2. Negative Sentences**
 
 We use "could not" when talking about something someone was **unable to do at a specific time in the past**.
 
 The sentence structure followed is,

 I/ You/ He/ She/ It/ They/ We/ any noun + **could not + verb (I form)**
 
 ```
 Examples
 
 I could not finish my homework because I was too tired.
 
 He could not lift the heavy box by himself.
 
 They could not attend the meeting because their flight was delayed.
 
 ```
 
 ###Polite Requests
 "Could" is also used to **make polite requests**. It is softer and more polite than using "can" and is often preferred in **formal** or polite situations.
 
 ```
 Examples
 
 Could you pass me the salt, please?
 
 Could you help me with this assignment?
 
 Could I leave a bit earlier today?
 
 ```
 
 ##Difference between “Can” and “Could”

| Modal Verb | Request Type | Example | Explanation |
|------------|--------------------------|--------------------------------------|-------------------------------------------------------------------|
| Can | Informal or Direct | "Can you help me with this task?" | "Can" is used for direct or informal requests. It’s often used with friends, family, or in casual situations where formality is not required. |
| Could | Polite or Formal | "Could you help me with this task?" | "Could" is used for polite or formal requests. It is softer and shows respect, making it appropriate for situations where you want to be more courteous, such as when speaking to a stranger, a teacher, or in a professional setting. |


#Shall
"Shall" is used to talk about something we plan to do or to make polite offers and suggestions. 

##”Shall” for Assertion

When we use "shall" to make an assertion, we are telling someone about something we are sure will happen or something we are promising to do in the future. 

###Structure

**1. Positive Sentences**
 
 ```
 I/ You/ She/ He/ It/ They/ We/ any noun + shall + verb (I form)
 
 ```
 
 **Examples**
 
 - I **shall call** you tomorrow.
 - We **shall complete** the project by Friday.
 - I **shall finish** my homework before dinner.
 
 **2. Negative Sentences**
 
 ```
 I/ You/ She/ He/ It/ They/ We/ any noun + shall not + verb (I form)
 ```
 
 **Examples**
 - I **shall not be** late to the meeting.
 - We **shall not allow** any distractions during the study session.
 - He **shall not forget** to bring the documents tomorrow.
 
 ##Shall for Requests
 
 "Shall" can also be used to make **polite requests, offers, or suggestions**. This is usually done in the **form of a question**.
 
 **Examples**
 - **Shall** I help you with your bags?
 - **Shall** we have lunch at the new restaurant?
 - **Shall** I open the window to let in some fresh air?
 
 --- 
 
 #Should
 
 "Should" is a modal verb that we use to **give advice, make recommendations, or state what we think is the right thing to do**. It can also be used politely to make **requests**.
 
 ##Should: For Assertion
 
 When we use "should" to make an assertion, we are giving advice or stating what we believe is the correct action.
 
 ###Structure
 
 **1. Positive Sentences**
 
 ```
 I/ You/ She/ He/ It/ They/ We/ any noun + should + verb (I form)
 
 ```
 
 **Examples**
 
 - You **should eat** more vegetables.
 - He **should exercise** regularly.
 - We **should visit** our grandparents more often.
 
 **2. Negative Sentences**
 
 ```
 I/ You/ She/ He/ It/ They/ We/ any noun + should not + verb (I form)
 
 ```
 
 **Examples**
 
 - You **should not eat** too much junk food.
 - He **should not stay up** too late if he has school tomorrow.
 - We **should not ignore** our responsibilities.
 
 
 ##Should: For Requests
 
 We use "should" to make **polite suggestions or requests**. It’s softer and shows consideration, often used when we want to offer help or ask for something in a polite manner.
 
 **Examples**
 
 **Should** we go for a walk after dinner?
 **Should** I call you later to discuss the plan?
 **Should** I bring anything for the picnic?
 
 -----
 
 #Must
 
 “Must" is a powerful modal verb that we use to **express something that is necessary** or very important. It shows a strong sense of obligation, requirement, or certainty. 
 
 When we use "must" for strong assertion, we are saying that something is essential or absolutely required. It indicates that the **action is not optional; it is a definite need**.
 
 ##Structure
 
 ###1. Positive Sentences
 
 ```
 I/ You/ She/ He/ It/ They/ We/ any noun + **must + verb (I form)**
 
 ```
 
 **Examples**
 
 - You **must wear** a seatbelt while driving.
 - He **must submit** the report by tomorrow.
 - We **must finish** this project on time.
 
 ###2. Negative Sentences


```
 I/ You/ She/ He/ It/ They/ We/ any noun + **must not + verb (I form)**
 
```
 
 **Examples**
 - You **must not use** your phone during the exam.
 - Children **must not play** near the road.
 - He **must not forget** to lock the door when leaving.


 #Will and Would

##Will: Express Intent

"Will" is often used to express intent, which means **showing a clear decision, plan, or determination to do something in the future**. It indicates a strong personal decision or purpose.

###Structure
####1. Positive Sentences

 ```
 I/ You/ She/ He/ It/ They/ We/ any noun + will + verb (I form)
 
 ```
 **Examples**
 - I **will start** exercising every day.
 - She **will finish** her homework before dinner.
 - We **will visit** our grandparents this weekend.
 
 ####2. Negative Sentences
 
 ```
 I/ You/ She/ He/ It/ They/ We/ any noun + **will not + verb (I form)**

 ```
 
 **Examples**

 - I **will not be** attending the meeting tomorrow.
 - She **will not eat** junk food anymore.
 - They **will not allow** anyone to enter the restricted area.
 
 ##Will: For Requests
 
 "Will" is often used to **make requests** in a direct yet polite way. It is **less formal than using "could"** but still polite and commonly used in everyday conversation.
 
 **Examples**
 
 - **Will** you help me move this table?
 - **Will** you please close the door?
 - **Will** you come to my party this weekend?
 
 ---
 
 ##Would: Express Intent
 
 When we use "would" to show intent, it means we are talking about **something we plan or want to do, but in a polite or less direct way**. 
 
 It's not as strong as saying "will," which shows definite plans. Instead, "would" softens the statement and makes it sound like a preference or a possible future action.
 
 ###Structure
 
 **1. Positive Sentences**
 
 ```
 I/ You/ She/ He/ It/ They/ We/ any noun + would + verb (I form)
 
 ```
 
 **Examples**
 
 - I **would like** to visit my grandparents next summer.
 - She **would prefer** to have tea instead of coffee.
 - They **would buy** a new house if they had enough money.
 
 **2. Negative Sentences**
 
 **"Would not"** is used to show a **refusal, unwillingness, or the absence of intention to do something**. 
 
 It’s softer than saying "won’t" and often shows reluctance or preference against doing something.
 
 ```
I/ You/ She/ He/ It/ They/ We/ any noun + would not + verb (I form)
 
 ```
 
 **Examples**
 
 - She **would not agree** to the proposal without more details.
 - They **would not support** the project unless it benefits the community.
 - I **would not move** to another city unless I found a better job opportunity.
 
 ##Would: For Requests
 
 When we use "would" for requests, **we are asking someone to do something**, but in a very polite and gentle way. It’s not as direct as asking with "can" or "will." Using "would" makes the request **softer and shows respect**.
 
 **Examples**
 
 - **Would you** mind closing the window?
 - **Would you** please pass me the salt?
 - **Would you** help me with this homework?
