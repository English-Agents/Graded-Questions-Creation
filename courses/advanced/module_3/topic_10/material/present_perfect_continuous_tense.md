# Present Perfect Continuous Tense

#Present Perfect Continuous

##1. Definition

The present perfect continuous tense is used to talk about actions that **started in the past and are still happening now**. 


##2. Structure

###2.1 Positive Sentence

**Using “has been”**

He/ She/ It/ any singular noun/ this/ that + **has been + verb +ing**

```
 Examples:
 
 He has been reading the book all day.
 
 She has been working at the company for 3 years.
 
 It has been raining for an hour.
 
 ```
 
 **Using “have been”**
 
 I/you/ we/ any plural noun/ those/ these + **have been + verb + ing**
 
 ```
 
 Examples:
 
 They have been playing football all afternoon.
 
 We have been watching the movie for two hours.
 
 I have been learning English for five years.
 
 ```
 
 ###2.2 Negative Sentences
 
 **Using “has been”**
 
 He/ She/ It/ any singular noun/this/ that + has not been + verb +ing
 
 ```
Examples:

He has not been studying for the exam.

She has not been feeling well lately.
 
It has not been snowing this winter.
 
 ```
 
 **Using “have been”**
 
I/you/ we/ any plural noun/ those/ these + **have not been + verb + ing**
 
 ```
Examples:
 
They have not been listening to the teacher.

We have not been traveling much this year.

I have not been sleeping well lately.
 
 ```


 ##Present Perfect Continuous: Scenario Differentiation

###1. Present Perfect Continuous (has/have been + verb (ing) ) and Present Perfect (has/have + verb (III form)

| **Aspect** | **Present Perfect (has/have + V3)** | **Present Perfect Continuous (has/have + been + V-ing)** |
|-----------------------------------|-------------------------------------------------------------------|------------------------------------------------------------------------|
| *Structure* | Subject + has/have + verb (III form) | Subject + has/have + been + verb-ing |
| *Focus* | Completion of an action with relevance to the present | Duration or ongoing nature of the action with relevance to the present |
| *Time Reference* | Refers to an action completed before now, with present relevance | Refers to an action that started in the past and is still continuing |
| *Example 1* | They have traveled to many countries. | They have been traveling for the last month. |
| | The action of traveling to many countries has been completed at some unspecified time before now, and it is relevant to the present. | They started traveling a month ago, and they are still traveling, with a focus on the duration of the journey. |
| *Example 2* | I have finished my report. | I have been working on my report all day. |
| | The report was completed at some point in the past, and the completion is relevant now, perhaps because the report is ready. | The speaker started working on the report earlier today and has been working on it continuously or repeatedly, with emphasis on the duration or effort involved. |
| *Example 3* | She has cleaned the kitchen. | She has been cleaning the kitchen. |
| | This indicates that the action of cleaning the kitchen was completed at some unspecified point before now, and the kitchen is clean now. | This suggests that she started cleaning the kitchen in the past, and she is still in the process of cleaning. The focus is on the duration or ongoing action. |

###2. Present Perfect Continuous (has/have been + verb (ing) ) and Past Simple (verb II form)

| **Aspect** | **Present Perfect Continuous (has/have + been + V-ing)** | **Past Simple (Subject + V2)** |
|-----------------------------------|-----------------------------------------------------------------------|---------------------------------------------------------------------|
| *Structure* | Subject + has/have + been + verb-ing | Subject + past form of verb (V2) |
| *Focus* | Duration or ongoing nature of the action with relevance to the present| Action completed in the past at a specific time |
| *Time Reference* | Refers to an action that started in the past and is still continuing, or was recently completed | Refers to a completed action in the past with a specific time (e.g., yesterday, last year) |
| *Example 1* | I have been reading this book for two hours. | I read that book yesterday. |
| | The speaker started reading the book two hours ago and is still reading it.. The focus is on the ongoing duration or process of reading. | The speaker completed the action of reading the book at a specific point in the past. The action is finished, and there's no connection to the present.|
| *Example 2* | She has been studying all day. | She studied yesterday. |
| | She started studying at some point earlier in the day and has been continuously studying. The focus is on the duration of the study session. | The action of studying was completed at a specific time in the past (yesterday). The focus is on the fact that she studied, with no emphasis on how long it lasted. |
| *Example 3* | They have been working on the project for a week. | They worked on the project last week. |
| | This shows that they started working on the project a week ago and have been working on it continuously. The focus is on the duration or ongoing process. | This indicates that the action of working on the project occurred at a specific time in the past (last week), and it is now complete. There's no direct connection to the present. |


###3. Present Perfect Continuous (has/have been + verb (ing) ) and Past Continuous (was/ were + Verb (ing form))

| **Aspect** | **Present Perfect Continuous (has/have + been + V-ing)** | **Past Continuous (was/were + V-ing)** |
|-----------------------------------|-----------------------------------------------------------------------|---------------------------------------------------------------------|
|*Structure* | Subject + has/have + been + verb-ing | Subject + was/were + verb-ing |
| *Focus* | Duration or ongoing nature of the action with relevance to the present| Ongoing action or temporary situation in the past, often interrupted |
| *Time Reference* | Refers to an action that started in the past and continues up to the present | Refers to an action happening at a specific point in the past, usually with a time reference |
| *Example 1* | I have been running for an hour. | I was running when it started raining. |
| | This suggests that I started running an hour ago and have been running continuously. The focus is on the ongoing nature or duration of running. | This indicates that the action of running was happening at a specific time in the past when something else (rain) occurred. |
| *Example 2* | He has been playing guitar all afternoon. | He was playing guitar when I called him. |
| | This suggests that he started playing guitar earlier today and has been playing it continuously. The focus is on the ongoing nature of the activity. | This indicates that he was in the middle of playing guitar at a specific time in the past (when I called), with an emphasis on what was happening during that time. |
| *Example 3* | They have been traveling for two weeks. | They were traveling when they met their friends. |
| | This suggests that they started traveling two weeks ago and have been traveling continuously. The focus is on the duration or ongoing nature of the travel. | This indicates that they were in the middle of traveling at a specific time in the past (when they met their friends), with an emphasis on what was happening during that time. |
