# Conjunctions

##Conjunctions 
*Conjunctions are words that are used to connect other words, phrases, and sentences to make compound sentences.*



**Example**: I want good grades, *so* I work hard.

###Types of Conjunctions



####Coordinating Conjunctions


*Coordinating conjunctions are words that connect words, phrases, or clauses of equal importance in a sentence, often linking two independent clauses.* 

Think of them as bridge words that help combine similar parts of a sentence to make it flow better. The most common ones can be remembered with the acronym FANBOYS.


FANBOYS

**For (reason)**
**And (addition)**
**Nor (negative addition)**
**But (contrast)**
**Or (choice)**
**Yet (another way to show contrast)**
**So (result)**


*Examples*



| Conjunctions | Indication                                            | Example 1                                  | Example 2                               |
|--------------|------------------------------------------------------|--------------------------------------------|-----------------------------------------|
| for          | used to indicate the reason or the purpose     | He decided to leave early, for he wanted to avoid the traffic.| I didn’t go to the party, for I was feeling quite ill.|
| and          | used to join similar or related words/phrases/clauses| She finished her homework, and she went out to play.| I will have a sandwich and a salad for lunch.|
| nor          | used to connect multiple negative alternatives       | They were not interested in the proposal, nor were they willing to negotiate.   | He did not study for the exam, nor did he complete his homework.|
| but          | used to show contrast                                 | He wanted to go to the party, but he had to finish his homework.| He trained very hard for the race, but he did not win.|
| yet          | used to express contrasting or unexpected information| It is raining, yet he refuses to take an umbrella.| The sun was shining brightly, yet it was surprisingly cold outside.|
| or           | used to connect words, phrases, or clauses that present alternatives or choices. It is one of the most common conjunctions and helps to show that either one option or another is possible or available.
                    | Would you like tea or coffee?               | We can go to the park, or we can visit the museum.|
| so           | used to show cause-and-effect relationships           | The sun was shining, so we went for a walk. | I forgot my umbrella, so I got wet in the rain.|


###More examples

*I wanted to go for a walk, but it started raining.*
*He bought apples and oranges from the market.*
*We can go to the beach, or we can stay home and watch a movie.*
*She didn't like the movie, nor did she enjoy the popcorn.*

**Point to Remember**

The conjunction "or" can also be used to indicate a condition, often presenting a consequence if a certain action is not taken or a certain condition is not met. In such cases, "or" connects the conditional action with its potential result.


e.g.
**Hurry up, or you will miss the bus.**

Here, "or" joins two clauses where the second clause presents a consequence of not fulfilling the condition stated in the first clause.


# Correlative Conjunction

*Correlative conjunctions are pairs of words that work together to connect parts of a sentence that are equally important.*

Think of them as team players that always come in pairs to join ideas. For example, in "both/and," both words work together to link two things or actions.

Here are a few pairs:

*both/and: Both the cat and the dog are cute.*
*either/or: You can either have pizza or pasta for dinner.*
*neither/nor: Neither the blue shirt nor the red shirt fits me.*

##Subordinating Conjunctions

*A subordinating conjunction is a word or phrase that connects a dependent clause to an independent clause. The dependent clause, introduced by the subordinating conjunction, relies on the independent clause to provide a complete thought or meaning.*

Subordinating conjunctions indicate relationships of time, cause and effect, contrast, condition, and more. 

Common subordinating conjunctions include words like "because," "although," "since," "if," "while," and "when." 

For example, in the sentence "Although it was raining, they went for a walk," the word "although" is a subordinating conjunction that links the dependent clause "it was raining" to the independent clause "they went for a walk."

# Conjunctive Adverbs

*Conjunctive adverbs are words that connect two independent sentences or clauses. They help show the relationship between the two sentences or clauses by indicating things like cause and effect, contrast, addition, or sequence.*



###Examples of Conjunctive Adverbs

| Conjunctive Conjunctions | Example 1 | Example 2 |
| ------------------------ | --------- | --------- |
| therefore | She studied hard for the exam; therefore, she passed with flying colors. | The company was not performing well; therefore, they decided to restructure. |
| furthermore | The project is behind schedule; furthermore, we are over budget. | She is an excellent cook; furthermore, she enjoys experimenting with new recipes. |
| moreover | The new policy will save money; moreover, it will improve efficiency. | The book is well-written; moreover, it provides a fresh perspective on the topic. |
| besides | She is a great leader; besides, she is very approachable. | The location is ideal; besides, the rent is affordable. |
| similarly | The first experiment yielded positive results; similarly, the second one was successful. | The company has grown rapidly; similarly, its competitors have also expanded. |
| likewise | She completed her project on time; likewise, her colleague met the deadline. | The team worked late into the night; likewise, the support staff stayed to assist them. |
| nevertheless | The journey was long and tiring; nevertheless, it was worth it. | The proposal had some flaws; nevertheless, it was accepted. |
| otherwise | We need to leave now; otherwise, we will miss the train. | Please follow the instructions; otherwise, you might get lost. |
| conversely | The medication worked well for her; conversely, it had no effect on her brother. | She excelled in the theoretical exam; conversely, she struggled with the practical test. |
| naturally | She excelled in her performance; naturally, she received a standing ovation. | The team worked hard; naturally, they achieved great results. |
| thus | The experiment was a success; thus, the hypothesis was confirmed. | She forgot her keys; thus, she was locked out of her house. |
| definitely | The results were impressive; definitely, our hard work paid off. | We need to reconsider our strategy; definitely, the current plan isn't working. |
| for instance | Many animals are nocturnal; for instance, owls and bats. | Some people are allergic to certain foods; for instance, peanuts and shellfish. |
| namely | Three students were absent, namely John, Sarah, and Tim. | She has lived in several countries, namely France, Italy, and Spain. |
| indeed | It was a difficult task; indeed, it was almost impossible. | He is an excellent speaker; indeed, he won several awards for his speeches. |
| of course | He knew the answer, of course. | Of course, you are invited to the party. |
| i.e. | She is a polyglot, i.e., a person who knows and can speak several languages. | The company needs to address its core issues, i.e., poor management and lack of funding. |
| however | She was very tired; however, she continued to work on her project. | He is a good player; however, he needs more practice. |
| subsequently | He graduated from college; subsequently, he started his own business. | The meeting was postponed; subsequently, it was rescheduled for next week. |
| for example | Many fruits are rich in vitamins; for example, oranges and strawberries. | There are several types of renewable energy; for example, solar and wind power. |
