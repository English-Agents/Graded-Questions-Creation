# Prepositions

#Prepositions

A preposition is a word that shows the relationship between a noun (or pronoun) and another word in a sentence. It often indicates location, time, direction, or manner.

##Types of Prepositions

1. **Preposition of Place** :  indicates the location or position of an object or a person.



    **Examples:**

```
The book is in the backpack.
The cat is on the roof.
The keys are under the mat.
I am sitting between two friends.
The lamp is beside the bed.
The car is parked behind the house.
```




2. **Preposition of Movement** :  indicates the direction or movement of an object or a person from one place to another.
    

  

**Examples:**

```
I am going to the store.
He came from the airport.
They swam across the river.
We walked through the forest.
She climbed over the wall.
The cat jumped into the box.
```

3. **Preposition of Manner**: describes how something is done or the method by which something occurs. It shows the way or style in which an action is performed.

    Common Prepositions of Manner:** *by, with, in, on*

    **Examples:**
    
    ```
She sang the song with enthusiasm.
He solved the problem by using a calculator.
They completed the task through teamwork.
She spoke to the audience in a confident manner.
The artist painted the picture in a realistic style.
He won the game by playing strategically.
She danced with grace and elegance.
  ```
  
4. **Preposition of Time**: indicates when something happens or for how long. It shows the relationship of time between different actions or events.

    Common Prepositions of Time: *at, on, in, during, by, before, after, since, until*
    
    **Examples**
    
   ``` 
I will meet you at 2 PM.
She was born on May 15th.
We have class in the morning.
I have been waiting for you since morning.
The concert is scheduled for Friday night.
I have a meeting from 3 PM to 5 PM.
We will finish the project by the end of the week.
```

**Avoid Unnecessary Prepositions**

In some cases, some verbs do not require a preposition.

In the sentence 'She visited her parents,' no preposition is needed because 'visited' directly tells us who she went to see (her parents). We do not need any extra word like 'to' or 'for' to show the relationship. 

If we say 'She visited to her parents,' the preposition 'to' would be unnecessary and incorrect. You only need 'visited her parents' to make sense.


| **Verb**   | **✅ Correct Sentence**                                          | **❌ Wrong Sentence**                                     |
|------------|------------------------------------------------------------------|---------------------------------------------------------|
| **Enter**  | We entered the room.                                             | We entered into the room.                               |
| **Approach**| I approached the teacher.                                       | I approached to the teacher.                            |
| **Discuss**| They discussed the issue.                                        | They discussed about the issue.                         |
| **Resemble**| She resembles her mother.                                       | She resembles to her mother.                            |
| **Consider**| I considered the offer.                                         | I considered on the offer.                              |
| **Influence**| He influenced the decision.                                     | He influenced on the decision.                          |
| **Marry**  | She married a lawyer. 
 She married someone she met at college | She married with/ to a lawyer. 
 She married with/ to someone she met at college |
| **Order**  | They ordered food.                                               | They ordered for food.                                  |
| **Go**     | Let's go swimming. 
 We should go shopping tomorrow. 
 Let's go camping. 
 I went shopping yesterday.

 She told me the news. | Please tell to us the story of your trip.

**Ending sentences with Prepositions**

Ending sentences with prepositions is common in modern English. 


While formal or traditional English might avoid ending sentences with prepositions, it is widely accepted in everyday spoken and written English.

**Examples**

- Who do these keys belong to?
- Is this the chair you were sitting on?
- What are you looking at?
- What kinds of things are you interested in?
- I am not sure what you are referring to.
- I would like to know where she comes from.
- He’s the person I work with.
- I wonder what kind of world we live in.
- I have no one to socialize with.
- I have no one to spend time with.
- The car had not been paid for.
- You've got nothing to be ashamed of.
- What kind of music do you listen to?



#Application of Prepositions

Some verbs and adjectives are commonly followed by specific prepositions. These combinations help to explain what the verb or adjective is acting on, or how it relates to other things in the sentence.

Note: There is no consistent rule or pattern for their usage. These combinations have to be learned through practice and exposure.

Some adjectives may use different prepositions in different contexts, and the meaning changes based on which preposition is used. So, it's important to consider the full context.

There are many verbs in English that are commonly used with prepositions to form verb + preposition combinations and these combinations convey a meaning different from the original verb. 

**Example:**

> 'Look up': 'Look' means to see something, but 'look up' means to search for something (like looking in a dictionary).

> - I need to look up the definition of this word in the dictionary.
> - Can you look up the train schedule for me?


| **Combination** | **Meaning** | **Examples** |
|-----------------|-------------|--------------|
| **Look into**   | To investigate, or to explore something in detail. | "I will look into the issue and get back to you." <br> "When we looked into buying a car, we decided it was better to hire one occasionally." |
| **Grow up**     | Grow older and become an adult. | "She grew up in Boston." <br> "Their children have all grown up and left home now." |
| **Hang out**    | Casually spend time at a particular place. | "We decided to hang out at the park this afternoon." <br> "They spent the whole day hanging out by the pool." |
| **Calm down**   | Relax | "You need to calm down before you can think clearly." <br> "She tried to calm down after hearing the bad news." |

|  | **Example 1** | **Example 2** |
|---|---------------|---------------|
| *Ending Sentences with Such Combinations* | She wants to be a doctor when she grows up. | Do you want to hang out?|
| *Ending Sentences with Prepositions, After Such Combinations* | This is the neighborhood I grew up in. | I have no one to hang out with. |
