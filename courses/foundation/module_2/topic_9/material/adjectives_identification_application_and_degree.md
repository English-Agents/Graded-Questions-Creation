# Adjectives: Identification, Application and Degree

#Adjectives: Identification

**What are Adjectives?**

An adjective is a word that describes a noun or a pronoun.

####Examples
```
Red apple
Yellow mango
Smart student
Cozy blanket
Ancient ruins
Green grass
Friendly cat
Delicious pizza
```





## Adjective Category

There are nine types of adjectives.

####They are:

- **Age** - e.g. New, Old, Ancient, Young, Modern, etc.
- **Color** - e.g. Blue, Pink, Pale, Black, Bright, Chocolate, etc.
- **Shape** - e.g. Round, Spherical, Rectangle, Triangular, etc.
- **Origin** - e.g. Indian, Telugu, Norwegian, Thai, Japanese, etc.
- **Make up** - e.g. Woollen, Wooden, Leather, Diamond, Cotton, etc.
- **Articles/Quantity/Numbers** - e.g. An, Some, Twenty, Whole, Much, etc.
- **Size** - e.g. Tiny, Giant, Big, Gigantic, etc.
- **Factual Quality or Opinion** - e.g. Good, Fascinating, Exciting, Impressive, etc.
- **Other Types** or **Noun Modifiers** - e.g. Table (fan), Arm (chair), Dining (table), etc.



#####More examples
```

Zigzag road

Woollen sweater

Drinking water
```

# Adjective: Degree

## Three types

- **Positive degree**
  - *Definition*: No comparison at all.
  - *Examples*: tall, rich, beautiful, fast, deep, good, delicious etc.

- **Comparative degree**
  - *Definition*: Comparing two
  - *Examples*: taller, more beautiful, faster, deeper, better, more delicious etc.

- **Superlative degree**
  - *Definition*: Comparing three or more things, pointing out the extreme quality that one of them has above all the others.
  - *Examples*: tallest, most beautiful, fastest, deepest, best, most delicious etc.
  
  
  
  ###Points to Remember

- We use *than* when comparing two nouns (i.e. in comparative degree).
  - *e.g. She is taller than her younger sister.*
  - *e.g. Cheetah is faster than any other land animal.*

- We use *the* when comparing many (i.e. in superlative degree).
  - *e.g. Mount Everest is the tallest mountain in the world.*
  - *e.g. Her chocolate cake recipe is the best I have ever tasted.*

- While many adjectives form their comparative degree by adding **-er** to the adjective *(e.g. taller, faster)*, others, especially the longer adjectives, use **more** before the adjective to indicate a comparison *(e.g. more beautiful, more interesting)*.

- While many adjectives form their superlative degree by adding **-est** to the adjective *(e.g. tallest, fastest)*, others, especially the longer ones, use **most** before the adjective to indicate the highest degree *(e.g. most beautiful, most interesting)*.
