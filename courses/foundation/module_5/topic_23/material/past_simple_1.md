# Past Simple 1

#Past Tense

**Definition**

The past tense is used to talk about actions, events, or situations that happened **before now**. It helps to clearly show that an action took place and was completed in the past, with no connection to the present.

**Example Sentences**

- He **watched** a movie last night.
- She **was** happy yesterday.
- We **used to go** to that school.

**Note:** We often use **time words** to show when the action happened, such as yesterday, last week, last year, in 1990, or two days ago.

Any moment that has passed can be considered as “past”. It can be both recent or late, like years/ months/ days or even minutes ago. 

**Recent Past: Example Sentences**

- I **just** finished my homework. (This action happened very recently, perhaps a few minutes ago.)
- She called me **an hour ago**. (The action of calling happened not too long ago.)
- They arrived at the airport **this morning**. (The arrival was recent, earlier today.)

**Late Past: Example Sentences**

- She graduated from college **ten years ago**. (The action of graduating happened a long time ago.)
- My grandparents moved to this city **in the 1970s**. (This event took place many years ago.)
- He visited Japan **when he was a child.** (This action occurred a long time ago in his childhood.)


##Past Simple: Information about a Completed Past Action

####Definition

The past simple tense is used to talk about actions or events that were completed at a specific time in the past. It shows that something happened before now, and it is no longer happening. 

###Sentence Structure

###1. Positive Sentences

The basic structure for forming a sentence in the past simple tense to inform about a completed action is 

```
I/ you/ we/ any plural noun/singular noun/ they/that/ this/those/these/ he/ she/ it + verb (II form) + rest of the sentence.
```
**Note:** Main verbs **do not change** form depending on the noun, in the case of simple past tense. 

**Example Sentences**

- The teacher **explained** the lesson clearly.
- He **bought** a new book two days ago.
- She **read** the entire book last night.

**Note:** The V2 form of verbs changes depending on the type of verb. For some, you simply add "-ed" to show the past, like “walk” changes to “walked” in its V2 form. Others change completely, like "go" becomes "went." A few verbs stay the same, such as "cut" remains "cut."

**Past Tense in Everyday Conversation**

- A: Why didn’t you come to the party?
B: I **missed** it because I **felt** sick.

- A: How was the concert?
B: It was fantastic! The band **played** all their hit songs..

--- 

###2. Negative Sentences

Negative sentences in the past simple tense are used to indicate that an action **did not happen in the past**. 
The basic structure for forming a negative sentence in the past simple tense is 

```
I/ you/ we/ any plural noun/singular noun/ they/that/ this/those/these/ he/ she/ it + did not +  verb (I form) + rest of the sentence. 
```
**Note:** In negative simple past tense sentences, the verb form will always be V1 (base form) preceded by did not, irrespective of the noun. Similarly, “did” also does not change depending on the noun.

**Example Sentences**

- She **did not attend** the meeting yesterday..
- They **did not finish** the project on time.
- I **did not eat** breakfast this morning.

**Everyday Conversation Examples**

- A: She **did not come** to the party last night.
B: Yeah, I noticed. She said she wasn’t feeling well.

- A: Did you see the new movie that came out last weekend?
B: "No, I **did not see it**. I was busy with work all weekend.

- A: They **did not order** any food for lunch.
B: Really? I thought they were planning to get some pizza.



##3. Sentences that Emphasize on the Action

###Definition

Sometimes we use "did" to **emphasize an action** in the simple past tense. It adds extra stress or confirmation to the fact that the action really occurred. 

This form is useful when we want to make the statement **more forceful and convey certainty**. It is also useful to reassure someone that the action was indeed performed. 

###Sentence structure

I/ you/ we/ any plural/singular noun/ they/that/ this/those/these/ he/ she/ it + *did + verb (I form)*

```
Point to Remember: When using "did" for emphasis, the main verb always stays in V1 form, 
even though it refers to a past action.
```

**Example Sentences**

- We *did arrange* the chairs for the event.
Explanation: "Did" is used here to confirm that the chairs were actually set up for the event, perhaps because someone said they weren’t arranged.

- They *did discover* a new hiking trail.
Explanation: By adding "did," the statement emphasizes that they really found a new trail, which helps to assure someone who might be doubtful.

- I *did suggest* a different plan for the trip.
Explanation: Here, "did" is used to stress that you actually made the suggestion, possibly in response to someone saying you didn’t offer any ideas.

**Everyday Conversation Examples**

- A: Are you sure you cleaned your room?
B: Yes, I **did clean** my room. You can check if you don't believe me.
Explanation: "Did clean" emphasizes that the action of cleaning actually happened, especially because the person is being doubted.

- A: It seems like you didn’t water the plants.
B:  I **did water** them this morning. Maybe they just need more water.
Explanation: The use of "did" emphasizes that the action of watering took place, correcting the assumption that it was not done.

- A: You probably didn't tell her about the change in plans.
B: I **did tell** her. I spoke to her just before lunch.
Explanation: "Did tell" emphasizes that the action of informing her actually took place, correcting the assumption that it wasn’t done.

###Difference between “V (II form)” and “did + V (I form)”

| Form | Use | Structure | Examples | Explanation |
| --- | --- | --- | --- | --- |
| V (II form) | States a past action or event without extra emphasis. | Any singular/plural noun + *verb (II form)* + rest of the sentence. | He fixed the broken chair. | This is a straightforward statement about what happened. |
| did + V (I form) | Adds emphasis, often to confirm, clarify, or correct a statement. | Any plural/singular noun + *did + verb (I form)* + rest of the sentence | He did fix the broken chair. | "Did fix" emphasizes that the action definitely took place, which is helpful if someone assumed the chair wasn't fixed. |
